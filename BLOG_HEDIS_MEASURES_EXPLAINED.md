## What Are HEDIS Measures, Really?

**Published:** November 2025

Your doctor tells you: "Your blood pressure is controlled."

Your organization tells you: "We closed the CBP measure for 47 patients this quarter."

Same outcome. Different language. One is clinical. One is financial.

**HEDIS measures are the financial language of quality.** They determine your Star rating. Your Star rating determines your revenue. Your revenue determines how much you can invest in care.

But here's what confuses most care teams: **HEDIS measures aren't some mysterious quality metric. They're simple, specific clinical outcomes that matter to patients.**

Understanding what they measure—and why—is the first step to improving them.

---

## The HEDIS Framework: Why It Matters

HEDIS stands for Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set. It's developed by the National Committee for Quality Assurance (NCQA).

**Think of it this way:**
- Medicare Advantage plans use HEDIS to measure quality
- CMS uses HEDIS to assign Star ratings
- Star ratings determine reimbursement rates
- Reimbursement determines organizational health

**One measure improves = one Star rating point improves = potentially $1M+ in additional revenue (for larger organizations)**

The big insurance companies (United, Humana, Aetna, Cigna) all measure using HEDIS. If you want to contract with them, if you want to compete for business, if you want to improve Star ratings—you're measuring HEDIS.

Currently, most organizations measure HEDIS manually:
- Care coordinators track patients
- Spreadsheets get updated
- Quarterly reports get generated
- Half the measure gaps are discovered too late to close

But the five core HEDIS measures below account for the majority of Star rating impact. Let's break down what each one actually measures.

## The Five Core HEDIS Measures

### 1. CDC: Comprehensive Diabetes Care

**What it measures:** Are we controlling diabetes effectively?

Specifically, CDC has four components:
- **HbA1c testing:** Did the patient get their HbA1c checked? (If not checked, can't manage)
- **HbA1c control:** Is the patient's HbA1c below 8%? (Good control prevents complications)
- **Blood pressure control:** Is the patient's BP below 140/90? (Diabetics with high BP = high cardiac risk)
- **ACE inhibitor/ARB:** Is the diabetic on an ACE inhibitor or ARB? (Gold standard for diabetic kidney protection)

**Why it matters:**
- 37 million Americans have diabetes
- Uncontrolled diabetes = $327 billion annual cost (complications)
- Every patient with controlled diabetes = $3K-$5K annual savings

**Impact on patients:**
- HbA1c below 8% = 40% fewer vision loss cases
- HbA1c below 8% = 50% fewer kidney failures
- HbA1c below 8% = 25% fewer amputations
- BP controlled = 35% fewer cardiac events

**How it's measured today (manual):**
1. Care coordinator identifies diabetic patients (checking EHR manually)
2. Reviews latest lab work (searching through multiple screens)
3. Checks BP readings (cross-referencing encounters)
4. Confirms medication list (pharmacy records)
5. Documents closure
6. Repeats for 500+ patients

**Time per patient:** 15-20 minutes  
**Annual effort:** 125-167 hours per organization  
**Staff cost:** $3K-$5K just for data entry

**Closure rate without automation:** 65-75% (some gaps found too late)

### 2. CBP: Controlling High Blood Pressure

**What it measures:** Are we controlling hypertension effectively?

**The metric:** Is the patient's BP below 140/90 (and documented)?

**Why it matters:**
- 45 million Americans have hypertension
- Uncontrolled BP = stroke, heart attack, kidney disease
- Controlled BP = $2K-$4K annual savings per patient

**Impact on patients:**
- BP controlled = 35% fewer strokes
- BP controlled = 40% fewer heart attacks
- BP controlled = 50% fewer kidney failures
- BP controlled = 30% fewer emergency department visits

**The measurement challenge:**
- Patient must have recent BP reading (visit in last 2 years)
- Reading must be documented in EHR
- Reading must be below threshold
- Many patients don't visit; many readings aren't documented

**Current gap identification:** Happens at year-end during measure reporting. Too late to intervene.

---

### 3. COL: Controlling High Cholesterol

**What it measures:** Are we managing lipid levels to prevent cardiovascular disease?

**The metric:** For patients with cardiovascular disease, is LDL cholesterol below 100 mg/dL?

**Why it matters:**
- 102 million Americans have high cholesterol
- High LDL = plaque buildup, heart attacks, strokes
- Controlled LDL = $1.5K-$3K annual savings per patient

**Impact on patients:**
- LDL controlled = 30% fewer heart attacks
- LDL controlled = 25% fewer strokes
- LDL controlled = consistent cardiovascular protection

**The measurement challenge:**
- Patient must have recent lipid panel (last 12 months)
- Must be on statin therapy (high-intensity or moderate + ezetimibe)
- Need to identify which patients actually have cardiovascular disease (not always documented as primary diagnosis)

---

### 4. BCS: Breast Cancer Screening

**What it measures:** Are we screening for breast cancer appropriately?

**The metric:** Women aged 40-74 with documented mammogram in last 24 months (or appropriate clinical reason for not screening)

**Why it matters:**
- Breast cancer kills 40,000+ women annually in the US
- Early detection = 90%+ 5-year survival
- Late detection = 25% 5-year survival
- Cost difference: $50K vs. $500K in treatment

**Impact on patients:**
- Regular screening = 20% reduction in breast cancer mortality
- Early detection = $450K savings in treatment costs per patient
- Peace of mind = health behavior improvement

**The measurement challenge:**
- Identify women in age range (some age data incomplete)
- Find documented mammograms (might be at outside facility)
- Distinguish appropriate non-screening (pregnancy, recent abnormality, palliative care)
- Track biennial intervals

**Current gap:** Many screening-eligible women slip through cracks because administrative staff don't flag them proactively.

---

### 5. CIS: Childhood Immunization Status

**What it measures:** Are children getting fully immunized?

**The metric:** Children 2 years old with complete immunization series (14 specific vaccines on schedule)

**Why it matters:**
- Vaccines prevent 21 million deaths over a lifetime
- Unvaccinated children = 95% infection risk for disease
- Cost per child: $1,500 (vaccines) vs. $10K+ per disease treated

**Impact on patients:**
- Fully vaccinated child = protection from 14 diseases
- Herd immunity protection for vulnerable populations
- $100K-$200K lifetime savings in prevention vs. treatment

**The measurement challenge:**
- Must verify 14 separate vaccines
- Must verify timing (on-schedule vs. delayed)
- Different tracking across pediatricians, schools, pharmacies, public health
- Catch-up vaccination pathways are complex

**Current gap:** Incomplete vaccine histories, non-documented vaccines (at other providers), difficulty tracking across organizations.

## Why Manual HEDIS Measurement Fails

Currently, most organizations track HEDIS measures manually:

**Year 1:**
- Quarter 1-3: Care coordinators manually identify gaps, close what they can
- Quarter 4: Annual reporting; discover gaps they missed
- **Actual closure rate:** 60-75%
- **Cost:** $15K-$25K in staff time
- **Lost revenue:** $100K-$300K (Star rating points not earned)

**The core problem:** Manual identification can't scale. You have 5 care coordinators. You have 8,000 patients. Manual review = 1-2 touches per patient per year. Gaps get missed.

## The Automated Solution: Real-Time HEDIS Tracking

Modern quality platforms can automate HEDIS measurement:

**What automation does:**

1. **Continuous identification:** System identifies every measure-eligible patient in real-time
   - No manual chart review
   - No missed patients
   - Immediate eligibility notification

2. **Gap detection:** For each eligible patient, system checks status
   - HbA1c done? If not, flag for lab order
   - BP controlled? If not, flag for medication adjustment
   - Cholesterol screened? If not, flag for lab order
   - Mammogram due? If not, flag for scheduling
   - Vaccines complete? If not, flag for provider intervention

3. **Automated workflows:** System triggers actions
   - Care coordinator gets notification: "John Smith needs HbA1c test"
   - Provider gets notification: "5 patients need BP medication adjustment"
   - Patient gets notification: "Your mammogram is due; schedule here"

4. **Outcome tracking:** System tracks closure progress
   - "CBP: 476/800 patients controlled = 59.5%"
   - "CDC HbA1c: 623/900 tested = 69%"
   - "BCS: 204/300 screened = 68%"
   - Real-time visibility into measure progress

**Impact on closure rates:**
- Manual: 60-75% closure rate
- Automated: 85-95% closure rate
- Difference: +10-20 percentage points = +$100K-$500K annual revenue per organization

**Impact on staff:**
- Manual: 1 hour per patient per year (identification + documentation)
- Automated: 10 minutes per patient per year (just closure documentation)
- 90% time savings

## Real Example: Diabetes Control (CDC Measure)

**Scenario:** 800-patient primary care practice with 15% prevalence of diabetes = 120 diabetic patients

### Manual Approach

**Staff task:** Find all diabetic patients, verify HbA1c control

1. **Identification:** Manual chart review to find all diabetics (3 hours)
2. **Lab verification:** Check latest HbA1c for each (2 hours, 20 patients/hour)
3. **BP verification:** Check latest BP reading (2 hours)
4. **Medication verification:** Confirm on ACE-I/ARB (1 hour)
5. **Closure:** Document gaps, coordinate closures (2 hours over 3 months)

**Total effort:** 10 hours per quarter = 40 hours per year = 1 staff FTE  
**Staff cost:** $25K-$35K annually  
**Closure rate:** 70% (84 of 120 controlled)  
**Star rating points earned:** 70  
**Revenue impact:** $70K-$100K annually

### Automated Approach

**System task:** Continuous CDC measure tracking

1. **Identification:** Automatic (diabetes diagnosis flag exists in system)
2. **Lab verification:** Automatic (EHR lab interfaces pull HbA1c real-time)
3. **BP verification:** Automatic (vital signs captured at visit)
4. **Medication verification:** Automatic (pharmacy data feeds, medication checking)
5. **Closure coordination:** System notifies care coordinator when gap detected

**Total effort:** 5 hours per quarter = 20 hours per year = 25% of 1 FTE  
**Staff cost:** $7K-$10K annually (care coordination only, no data entry)  
**Closure rate:** 92% (110 of 120 controlled)  
**Star rating points earned:** 92  
**Revenue impact:** $120K-$150K annually

**Annual benefit:** +$50K-$80K revenue + $18K-$25K staff savings = $68K-$105K total improvement
