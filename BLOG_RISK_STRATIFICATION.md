## Risk Stratification: Stop Wasting Resources on Low-Risk Patients

**Published:** November 2025

Your care coordination team spends time equally on all patients. High-risk patient gets the same number of outreach attempts as a low-risk patient. Both get the same intensity of intervention. Both consume the same care coordinator hours.

This is efficient to implement. It's also destroying your ROI.

**80% of your healthcare costs come from 20% of your patients.** That's not hyperbole—that's healthcare economics. A small percentage of high-risk, complex patients drive the majority of ED visits, hospitalizations, readmissions, and complications.

And right now, you're probably treating them the same as everyone else.

---

## The 80/20 Reality at Your Organization

Let's be concrete. If you manage 15,000 attributed patients:

**Low-Risk Patients (80% = 12,000 patients)**
- Average annual cost: $2,000/patient
- Total cost: $24M
- Care coordination time: Minimal
- Intervention intensity: Preventive

**Medium-Risk Patients (16% = 2,400 patients)**
- Average annual cost: $8,000/patient
- Total cost: $19.2M
- Care coordination time: Moderate
- Intervention intensity: Standard

**High-Risk Patients (4% = 600 patients)**
- Average annual cost: $35,000/patient
- Total cost: $21M
- Care coordination time: Currently minimal (same as low-risk)
- Intervention intensity: Currently standard (same as low-risk)

**The problem is obvious:** The 600 high-risk patients are generating $21M in costs—nearly 25% of total spend. But they're getting the same care coordination attention as the 12,000 low-risk patients.

Result: High-risk patients don't get enough focus. Low-risk patients get wasted attention. Resources aren't optimized.

## Current Risk Identification Doesn't Work

### Manual Assessment is Incomplete

Most organizations try manual risk assessment. Maybe quarterly, maybe annually. A clinician pulls a patient chart, reviews diagnoses, makes a judgment call: "Is this patient high-risk?"

Problems:
- Only happens when someone remembers to do it
- Biased (clinician judgment varies)
- Incomplete (mental health, social factors invisible)
- Not actionable (risk score with no intervention protocol)
- Can't predict who's about to decompensate

### EHR Risk Scoring is Limited

Your EHR might have built-in risk scoring. It looks at diagnoses, lab values, utilization. But:

- **Data limited:** Only sees what's in the EHR (misses mental health, social determinants, behavioral factors)
- **Siloed:** Not integrated with quality measures or care coordination workflows
- **Static:** Risk doesn't update in real-time as patient status changes
- **Non-actionable:** Score appears, but no intervention template. What do you do with a "high-risk" score?

### Third-Party Risk Tools are Expensive & Late

Some organizations buy third-party risk stratification (Optum, Humana, etc.). But:

- **Cost:** $3-5 PMPM ($450K-$750K annually for 15,000 patients)
- **Data lag:** 1-4 weeks old by the time you see it
- **Siloed:** Separate system from your quality measures
- **Data control:** Your data locked in vendor platform
- **Limited customization:** One-size-fits-all algorithm

**High-Risk Patient (Robert, 75yo, CHF + diabetes + COPD):**

- No home monitoring
- No weekly outreach
- Crisis happens → ED visit ($2,000) or hospitalization ($15,000)

**Low-Risk Patient (Jennifer, 40yo, well-controlled HTN):**
- Gets monthly check-in (unnecessary)
- Gets reminder calls (unnecessary)

- Consumes care coordinator time that could go to Robert

**Organization-wide impact:**
- High-risk patients decompensate → preventable ED visits and hospitalizations = $500K-$2M/year
- Low-risk patients over-contacted → care coordinator burnout, inefficiency
- Medium-risk patients neglected → gaps not closed, measures not improved
- Resource allocation: Completely backwards

## The Solution: Automated Risk Stratification

Real risk stratification is different. It's automated. It's continuous. It's tied to intervention protocols. It drives outcomes.

### How It Works

**Step 1: Continuous Risk Assessment**

System analyzes patient data automatically:
- **Clinical factors:** Diagnoses, lab values (HbA1c, ejection fraction), medication list
- **Mental health factors:** Depression, anxiety, substance use (now included)
- **Social determinants:** Housing, food security, transportation, social support
- **Utilization patterns:** ED visits, hospitalizations, readmissions (past 12 months)

Patient is assigned to tier: **Low / Medium / High**

**Step 2: Automated Prioritization**

Care coordinator dashboard shows patients by risk tier:
- **High-risk (top of screen):** Immediate attention
- **Medium-risk (middle):** Structured intervals
- **Low-risk (below fold):** Annual/quarterly check-in

Team knows immediately: "These 600 patients need 80% of my attention."

**Step 3: Tier-Specific Interventions**

**High-risk protocol:**
- Weekly outreach (vs. monthly for low-risk)
- Intensive case management
- Behavioral health integration
- Home monitoring equipment (BP cuff, scale, pulse ox)
- Early ED/hospitalization prevention

**Medium-risk protocol:**
- Bi-weekly outreach
- Standard care coordination
- Measure-specific interventions

**Low-risk protocol:**
- Quarterly or annual contact
- Self-management resources
- Preventive care reminders

**Step 4: Dynamic Reassessment**

Risk tiers update weekly. Patient improves? Moves to lower tier. Patient decompensates? Escalates to higher tier. Care model adapts in real-time.

## Real Impact: Robert Johnson's Story

**Before risk stratification:** Robert is 75, has CHF (EF 35%), diabetes, hypertension, COPD, depression. He lives alone with limited social support. Last year: 1 hospitalization, 2 ED visits.

Currently, he gets monthly check-ins (same as low-risk patients). No systematic monitoring. No behavioral health integration. No care coordination escalation.

**Outcome:** Crisis → ED visit ($2,000) or hospitalization ($15,000).

**Cost to organization (6 months): $20K+**

---

**With risk stratification:** Robert is automatically flagged as **HIGH-RISK** (multiple chronic diseases + recent ED/hospital + CHF + depression).

**Intervention protocol:**
- Weekly outreach (vs. monthly)
- Home BP monitor + scale (linked to system)
- Medication adherence support
- Behavioral health referral (depression management)
- Care coordination flag: "CHF exacerbation risk—watch for shortness of breath, weight gain, swelling"

**Outcomes (6 months):**
- 0 ED visits (vs. 1 typical)
- 0 hospitalizations (vs. 1-2 typical)
- Depression treatment started; compliance improving
- Medication adherence: 85% (vs. prior ~40%)
- CHF stable; patient reports better quality of life

**Cost to organization (6 months):**
- Prevented ED visit: -$2,000 saved
- Prevented hospitalization: -$12,000 saved
- Fewer complications: -$1,000 saved
- Total: **$15,000 saved in 6 months**

**Multiply by 600 high-risk patients:** 600 × $10K = **$6M organization-wide annual savings**
