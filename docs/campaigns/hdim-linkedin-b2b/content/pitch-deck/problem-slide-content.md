# The Problem: When Systems Fail, Patients Suffer

## A Healthcare System Working Against Its Own Mission

Every day, physicians and CMOs face an impossible reality: they became healers to help patients, but fragmented data systems and administrative barriers stand between them and the care their patients desperately need.

---

## Patient Care Barriers

### The Information Black Hole

- **Incomplete Patient Picture**: Over 270 million patient records are scattered across disconnected health information exchanges and data networks. Physicians routinely make critical decisions with only partial information about their patients' medical histories, medications, and prior treatments.

- **Time Lost to Data Hunting**: Primary care physicians spend only 40% of their time patient-facing, with 60% consumed by desktop medicine, inbox management, and chasing down information across systems. That's 3.2 hours per day lost to administrative activities instead of healing.

- **Care Gaps Hidden in Silos**: Despite 70% of hospitals engaging in some form of data exchange, 48% share data without receiving anything back. Critical gaps in preventive care, medication reconciliation, and follow-up treatment go undetected because the data simply isn't connected.

- **Prior Authorization Paralysis**: Physicians handle a median of 43 prior authorization requests weekly, consuming nearly 12 staff hours that could otherwise support patient care. 93% of physicians report that prior authorization delays necessary care, and 78% see patients abandon treatment entirely due to authorization struggles.

## The Human Cost: When Data Fails, Lives Are Lost

Behind every statistic is a patient who waited too long, a diagnosis that came too late, or a treatment that was delayed until the window of opportunity closed.

Nearly 24% of hospitalized patients experience at least one adverse event during their stay, with approximately 7% of all hospital admissions resulting in preventable harm. Medical errors remain the third leading cause of death in the United States, with estimates suggesting 200,000 to 400,000 preventable patient deaths annually. Poor communication--often rooted in fragmented information systems--contributes to two out of three serious, preventable adverse events.

Meanwhile, the healers themselves are breaking. 49% of physicians report burnout, with "too many bureaucratic tasks" cited as the number one contributor by 62% of doctors. More than 1 in 4 medical groups saw a physician leave or retire early due to burnout in 2024 alone. This isn't just a workforce crisis--it's a patient care crisis. Burned-out physicians make more errors, have less empathy reserve, and ultimately leave medicine when we need them most.

## The Bottom Line

**Healthcare's data fragmentation isn't just an IT problem--it's a patient safety crisis.**

Every disconnected system, every prior authorization delay, every hour spent hunting for patient information represents a moment when a physician couldn't do what they were trained to do: heal.

The cost isn't measured only in dollars (though $700+ billion in annual waste is staggering). The true cost is measured in:
- Lives lost to preventable errors
- Treatments abandoned because patients gave up fighting the system
- Physicians leaving medicine because they can no longer practice it meaningfully
- Trust eroded between patients and the healthcare system meant to serve them

**There has to be a better way.**
