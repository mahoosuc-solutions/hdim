# 5 HEDIS Measures Every ACO Must Master in 2025

*The quality measures that make or break your shared savings*

---

## The Quality Score That Pays (or Penalizes)

In MSSP and ACO REACH, your quality score isn't just a report card—it's a multiplier on your shared savings. Score in the bottom quartile, and you keep just 25% of what you've earned. Score in the top quartile, and you keep 100%.

For a typical ACO generating $2 million in gross savings, that's the difference between taking home $500,000 and taking home $2,000,000.

**The stakes have never been higher.**

HEDIS (Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set) measures form the backbone of quality evaluation across Medicare, Medicaid, and commercial value-based contracts. With 92+ measures in the full HEDIS specification, knowing where to focus is critical.

This guide breaks down the five HEDIS measures that have the biggest impact on ACO performance—and how to master each one.

---

## Measure 1: Controlling High Blood Pressure (CBP)

### Why It Matters

Hypertension affects 47% of American adults. Uncontrolled blood pressure is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and chronic kidney disease. For ACOs, it's also one of the highest-weighted quality measures.

- **Prevalence:** Nearly half your patient population
- **Impact:** $131 billion annual US healthcare cost
- **Quality Weight:** High priority in CMS and commercial programs

### The Measure

**Numerator:** Patients 18-85 with hypertension diagnosis whose most recent blood pressure is adequately controlled (< 140/90 mmHg)

**Denominator:** Patients 18-85 with hypertension diagnosis, with exclusions for:
- ESRD
- Kidney transplant
- Pregnancy
- Hospice

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Stale readings** - Using BP from months ago when more recent readings exist
2. **Documentation gaps** - BP taken but not recorded in structured fields
3. **Cuff calibration** - Home BP readings not validated
4. **White coat syndrome** - Office readings higher than actual control

### Mastery Strategies

| Strategy | Impact | Effort |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Real-time BP alerts at every visit | High | Low |
| Home BP monitoring programs | Very High | Medium |
| Pharmacist medication management | High | Medium |
| Automated patient outreach for gaps | High | Low |

**Target:** 75%+ control rate (top performers exceed 80%)

## Measure 2: Diabetes: Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) Poor Control (HBD)

### Why It Matters

Diabetes affects 37.3 million Americans and costs $327 billion annually. Poor glycemic control leads to preventable complications: blindness, amputations, kidney failure, and cardiovascular events.

This measure is scored **inversely**—you want the *lowest* rate of poor control.

### The Measure

**Numerator:** Patients 18-75 with diabetes whose most recent HbA1c is > 9.0% (poor control)

**Denominator:** Patients 18-75 with diabetes diagnosis

**Note:** This is a "lower is better" measure. Your goal is to minimize the percentage of patients with HbA1c > 9%.

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Missing labs** - Patients haven't had HbA1c tested in 12+ months
2. **Lab integration gaps** - Results exist but aren't in your system
3. **Engagement drop-off** - Patients with poor control avoid appointments
4. **Medication complexity** - Non-adherence drives poor outcomes

### Mastery Strategies

| Strategy | Impact | Effort |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Quarterly HbA1c reminders | High | Low |
| Pharmacist-led diabetes management | Very High | Medium |
| Continuous glucose monitoring | Very High | High |
| Care coordinator outreach for HbA1c > 9% | High | Medium |
| Endocrinology co-management | High | Medium |

**Target:** < 15% poor control rate (top performers < 10%)

## Measure 3: Colorectal Cancer Screening (COL)

### Why It Matters

Colorectal cancer is the second leading cause of cancer death in the US—yet it's highly preventable with screening. Early detection through screening reduces mortality by 60-70%.

For ACOs, this measure often has the most room for improvement because baseline rates are typically low.

### The Measure

**Numerator:** Patients 45-75 with appropriate colorectal cancer screening:
- Fecal occult blood test (FOBT) in past year
- FIT-DNA test in past 3 years
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy in past 5 years
- Colonoscopy in past 10 years
- CT colonography in past 5 years

**Denominator:** Patients 45-75, with exclusions for:
- Colorectal cancer history
- Total colectomy
- Hospice

**2025 Update:** Age range expanded to 45-75 (previously 50-75)

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Colonoscopy data gaps** - Procedure done elsewhere, not in your records
2. **Age expansion blind spots** - 45-49 population often overlooked
3. **Patient refusal** - "I'll do it next year" every year
4. **FIT kit completion** - Kits sent but not returned

### Mastery Strategies

| Strategy | Impact | Effort |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Mailed FIT kits with return envelope | Very High | Medium |
| Text/call reminders for kit return | High | Low |
| Colonoscopy navigation support | High | Medium |
| Direct-access colonoscopy scheduling | Very High | Medium |
| HIE data aggregation for external procedures | High | High |

**Target:** 70%+ screening rate (top performers exceed 80%)

## Measure 4: Breast Cancer Screening (BCS)

### Why It Matters

Breast cancer is the most common cancer among women (excluding skin cancer). Mammography screening reduces breast cancer mortality by 20-40% in women 50-74.

This measure is highly actionable—it's a single test, widely available, and patient-friendly compared to colonoscopy.

### The Measure

**Numerator:** Women 50-74 who had a mammogram in the past 27 months

**Denominator:** Women 50-74, with exclusions for:
- Bilateral mastectomy
- History of bilateral mastectomy
- Hospice

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Imaging center fragmentation** - Mammogram done at facility not in your network
2. **Result documentation** - Mammogram completed but results not captured
3. **Age boundary issues** - Patients aging into or out of measure
4. **Scheduling barriers** - Long wait times deter completion

### Mastery Strategies

| Strategy | Impact | Effort |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Automated mammogram reminders | High | Low |
| Pre-scheduled appointments at wellness visits | Very High | Low |
| Mobile mammography vans | Very High | High |
| Same-day mammogram availability | High | Medium |
| Community health worker outreach | High | Medium |

**Target:** 75%+ screening rate (top performers exceed 85%)

## Measure 5: Depression Screening and Follow-Up (DSF)

### Why It Matters

Depression affects 8.4% of adults and is a significant driver of healthcare utilization, treatment non-adherence, and poor chronic disease outcomes. It's also one of the fastest-growing measure categories in HEDIS.

Unlike the previous measures, DSF requires both screening AND follow-up action—making it operationally complex but high-impact.

### The Measure

**Numerator:** Patients 12+ screened for depression using a standardized tool (PHQ-2, PHQ-9, etc.) AND, if positive, have documented follow-up plan on the date of screening

**Denominator:** Patients 12+ with a qualifying encounter, with exclusions for:
- Active depression diagnosis with current treatment
- Bipolar disorder
- Hospice

**Follow-up options for positive screen:**
- Additional evaluation
- Suicide risk assessment
- Referral to practitioner
- Pharmacotherapy
- Other interventions

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Paper-based screening** - PHQ-9 forms lost or not entered
2. **Screening without follow-up** - Positive screen but no documented action
3. **Documentation gaps** - Follow-up happened but wasn't recorded properly
4. **Workflow inconsistency** - Some providers screen, others don't

### Mastery Strategies

| Strategy | Impact | Effort |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Tablet-based screening in waiting room | Very High | Medium |
| Automatic EHR scoring and alerts | Very High | Medium |
| Integrated behavioral health | Very High | High |
| Care coordinator same-day outreach | High | Medium |
| Warm handoff protocols | High | Medium |

**Target:** 75%+ screening with follow-up rate (top performers exceed 85%)

## Building Your HEDIS Strategy

### The 80/20 Rule for Quality Improvement

Not all measures are created equal. These five measures—CBP, HBD, COL, BCS, and DSF—typically account for a disproportionate share of:

1. **Quality score impact** - High weight in MSSP/ACO REACH
2. **Improvement opportunity** - Room to move from baseline
3. **Patient health impact** - Prevention of downstream costs

Mastering these five gives you the foundation for overall quality performance.

### Prioritization Framework

| Measure | Your Baseline | Improvement Potential | Priority |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|----------|
| CBP | _____% | High if < 75% | _____ |
| HBD (poor control) | _____% | High if > 15% | _____ |
| COL | _____% | High if < 70% | _____ |
| BCS | _____% | High if < 75% | _____ |
| DSF | _____% | Very High if < 50% | _____ |

### Implementation Phases

**Phase 1: Foundation (Month 1-2)**
- Establish accurate baseline rates
- Identify care gaps at patient level
- Set up tracking dashboards
- Train care teams on measure specifications

**Phase 2: Quick Wins (Month 3-4)**
- Implement automated reminders
- Close "easy" gaps (recent tests not captured)
- Deploy screening workflows
- Launch patient outreach campaigns

**Phase 3: Sustained Improvement (Month 5-12)**
- Care management for complex patients
- Integrate behavioral health
- Expand home monitoring programs
- Continuous optimization based on data

## Technology Requirements

### Data Aggregation

To accurately measure HEDIS performance, you need data from multiple sources:

| Data Source | Data Type | Importance |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| EHR | Labs, vitals, encounters | Critical |
| Labs | Quest, LabCorp, hospital labs | Critical |
| Imaging centers | Mammogram, colonoscopy results | High |
| HIE | External procedures and tests | High |
| Claims | Utilization patterns | Medium |

### Real-Time Dashboards

Static monthly reports don't drive improvement. You need:

- **Population view:** Overall rates by measure, trend over time
- **Provider view:** Individual performance vs. peers
- **Patient view:** Gap lists sortable by measure, priority, risk
- **Drill-down capability:** From population to individual patient

### Workflow Integration

Quality data must integrate with daily workflows:

- **Pre-visit planning:** Care gaps visible before appointment
- **Point-of-care alerts:** Reminders during visit
- **Task management:** Outreach assignments with tracking
- **Care coordination:** Handoffs between team members

## Common Questions

### How often should we measure HEDIS performance?

**Answer:** Continuously, with weekly or monthly reviews. Annual HEDIS reporting is the final measurement, but you should be tracking and improving throughout the year.

### What's a realistic improvement target?

**Answer:** Most organizations can improve 10-20 percentage points per year on focused measures. For example, moving CBP from 65% to 78% in 12 months is achievable with systematic effort.

### How do we handle patients attributed to us but seen elsewhere?

**Answer:** This is why data aggregation matters. Connect to HIEs, request records from external providers, and ensure your attribution lists are accurate.

### Should we focus on all HEDIS measures or just a few?

**Answer:** Start with your lowest-performing measures that have the highest weight. Trying to improve everything at once dilutes effort. Master the five core measures first.
