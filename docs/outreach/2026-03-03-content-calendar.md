## Post Outlines

### Post #4 — Data Latency vs. Data Availability (Mar 13)

**Thesis:** The healthcare industry solved the data availability problem (FHIR, HIEs, ADT feeds). It hasn't solved the data latency problem. Having the data isn't the same as having it in time to act.

**Key points:**
- FHIR adoption is accelerating. Most major health systems expose FHIR endpoints
- ADT notifications are now mandated. Admission/discharge data flows in near-real-time
- But quality measurement still runs on batch processes with quarterly or annual cycles
- The infrastructure to exchange data exists. The infrastructure to evaluate quality measures against that data in real time doesn't
- Analogy: having a thermometer that reads accurately but only once a day isn't useful for managing a fever

**Hashtags:** `#FHIR #Interoperability #HealthIT #QualityMeasures #DigitalHealth`
**Target groups:** HIE (#8), Healthcare IT (#9), FHIR & Interoperability (#11)
**Engagement targets:** John Halamka (FHIR), Edward Yurcisin (NCQA CTO), Health IT community

### Post #5 — Chart Chase Economics (Mar 18)

**Thesis:** The traditional HEDIS chart chase model is hitting diminishing returns. Yield rates are declining, costs per gap are increasing, and the chart review workforce is shrinking. The economics now favor investing in real-time data capture over retrospective evidence collection.

**Key points:**
- Chart chase yield rates have declined as easy-to-reach providers have been exhausted
- Cost per gap closure through chart review keeps increasing (provider office staffing changes, fax-based workflows)
- NLP/AI extraction from unstructured records helps but introduces accuracy trade-offs
- The underlying assumption — that clinical evidence exists but wasn't captured in structured data — is increasingly false. In many cases the evidence was captured digitally but never made it to the quality measurement system
- The economic crossover point: when real-time clinical data feeds become cheaper per gap than retrospective chart review

**Hashtags:** `#HEDIS #QualityMeasures #HealthcareOperations #ValueBasedCare #CareGaps`
**Target groups:** HEDIS Professionals (#1), NCQA & HEDIS (#4), Quality Improvement (#2)
**Engagement targets:** Courtney Breece (Inovalon), Daniel Allen (HEDIS consultant), Kimber Bishop

### Post #6 — Health Equity and Measurement Timing (Mar 20)

**Thesis:** Measurement latency disproportionately affects vulnerable populations. Members in fragmented care networks take longer to aggregate clinical data for, which means their care gaps are identified later and intervention windows are shorter.

**Key points:**
- Dual-eligible and low-income populations see more providers across more settings
- Clinical data for these members is more fragmented and takes longer to consolidate
- A 6-month data lag affects a member with one PCP differently than a member seeing 5 specialists across 3 health systems
- Health equity in quality measurement isn't just about adjusting for social risk factors in scoring. It's about ensuring the measurement infrastructure doesn't systematically disadvantage the populations that need the most support
- CMS health equity index in Stars creates financial incentive to solve this

**Hashtags:** `#HealthEquity #MedicareStars #SDOH #ValueBasedCare #QualityMeasures`
**Target groups:** Value-Based Care (#3), ACO Leaders (#10), Population Health (#16)
**Engagement targets:** Sachin Jain (SCAN), Amy Calderone (VBC), Stephanie Berger (MA Quality)

### Post #7 — Digital Quality Measures — ECDS Reality Check (Mar 25)

**Thesis:** NCQA's transition to ECDS-only measures is the right strategic direction but creates a "have vs. have-not" dynamic. Organizations with mature FHIR infrastructure will thrive. Organizations still dependent on claims and chart review face a capability cliff.

**Key points:**
- 3 measures transitioning to ECDS-only in HEDIS MY 2026, plus 6 new ECDS measures. More are coming each year — NCQA plans to retire hybrid reporting entirely by MY 2029
- ECDS requires structured clinical data from EHRs, health plans, and registries — not claims
- Large integrated health systems (Kaiser, Geisinger model) are well-positioned
- Fragmented payer-provider ecosystems face significant infrastructure gaps
- The transition timeline is clear. The readiness gap between organizations is not
- This isn't a technology problem that can be solved by buying a vendor product. It's a data architecture problem that requires rethinking how clinical data flows from point of care to quality measurement

**Hashtags:** `#HEDIS #ECDS #DigitalQuality #FHIR #NCQA #QualityMeasures`
**Target groups:** NCQA & HEDIS (#4), FHIR & Interoperability (#11), Healthcare IT (#9)
**Engagement targets:** Vivek Garg (NCQA CEO), Edward Yurcisin (NCQA CTO), Courtney Breece (Inovalon)

### Post #8 — What 2027 Stars Performance Requires Today (Mar 27)

**Thesis:** Stars ratings have a 2-year lag between quality performance and financial impact. Plans that want to improve 2027 Stars needed to change their measurement infrastructure in 2025. The plans starting now are already behind, but the ones that haven't started are in real trouble.

**Key points:**
- 2027 Star ratings are based on HEDIS MY 2025 performance (already locked)
- 2028 Stars reflect MY 2026 (currently in progress — this is the window)
- Plans optimizing for 2028+ need real-time quality measurement now, during MY 2026
- The compounding effect: plans that invest in measurement infrastructure see returns across multiple Stars years, not just one
- Historical pattern: the plans that invested early in HEDIS automation in the 2010s built durable Stars advantages that compounded over a decade
- The same dynamic is playing out now with real-time quality measurement. Early movers will build structural advantages

**Hashtags:** `#MedicareStars #MedicareAdvantage #HEDIS #QualityMeasures #ValueBasedCare`
**Target groups:** MA Stars & Quality (#5), Medicare Star Ratings (#6), Managed Care Executives (#12)
**Engagement targets:** Sarah Dagostino (UHC Stars), Mike Wilson (CalOptima), Randy Strite (consultant), Russ Kite
