## Payer-Side Quality Executives

### Sarah Dagostino — Director, STARs Health Advancement, UnitedHealthcare

**Context:** Manages Stars/HEDIS performance for the largest MA plan. Posts about Stars strategy, quality improvement programs, member outreach.

**Comment 1 (Stars strategy):**
The gap between identifying a care gap and having an actionable intervention window keeps shrinking. Plans that built their Stars strategy around annual chart review cycles are discovering those cycles can't keep pace with CMS methodology changes that reward real-time performance. Curious whether you're seeing the same compression on the supplemental data side.

**Comment 2 (Member outreach):**
One pattern I keep seeing is that outreach effectiveness drops sharply when the gap identification is more than 90 days old. By the time the call list is generated from claims, the member's clinical situation has often changed. The plans closing the most gaps are the ones shortening that identification-to-outreach latency.

**Comment 3 (HEDIS performance):**
What's interesting about the 2026 Stars recovery is how much came from CMS methodology adjustments versus actual quality improvement. The disaster provisions and cut point holds masked a real question: are plans actually getting better at closing gaps, or just benefiting from a more favorable scoring environment?

### Courtney Breece — VP Product, Inovalon (HEDIS & Stars)

**Context:** Builds HEDIS measurement software. Active thought leader on quality measurement modernization, digital quality measures, ECDS transition.

**Comment 1 (Digital quality/ECDS):**
The ECDS transition is where this gets real. Moving from hybrid to electronic-only measures sounds like a technology upgrade, but it's actually a fundamental shift in who owns the data pipeline. Plans that outsourced chart review can't outsource real-time clinical data feeds the same way. The infrastructure requirements are structurally different.

**Comment 2 (Quality measurement modernization):**
The part of quality measurement modernization that doesn't get enough attention is the timing problem. We've gotten much better at measuring accurately. We haven't gotten proportionally better at measuring quickly. A perfectly accurate HEDIS rate calculated 6 months after the measurement year is less useful than an 85%-accurate estimate available in real time.

**Comment 3 (HEDIS software/technology):**
One thing I appreciate about this perspective is the emphasis on workflow integration over reporting accuracy. Most quality measurement tools were built to produce a number at the end of the year. The next generation needs to produce a signal during the care encounter. That's a different product architecture entirely.

### Megan Cushion — Healthcare Quality Leader, CMS Stars & HEDIS Expert

**Context:** Manages $40M in value-based contracts. CPHQ-certified. Posts about VBC performance, quality certification, HEDIS operations.

**Comment 1 (Value-based contracts):**
Managing VBC contract performance at that scale requires closing the feedback loop between clinical operations and financial performance faster than most organizations are set up to do. The contracts that perform best seem to be the ones where quality data flows back to the care team in days, not quarters.

**Comment 2 (HEDIS operations):**
The HEDIS operational challenge that doesn't get discussed enough is the human bottleneck in supplemental data review. Chart chase yields are declining, NLP extraction is improving but imperfect, and the measurement year keeps getting more compressed. At some point the process needs to invert: instead of finding evidence after the fact, you need to capture it at the point of care.

**Comment 3 (CPHQ/quality certification):**
The certification emphasizes process improvement and measurement rigor, but the biggest operational challenge I see in quality programs isn't measurement accuracy. It's measurement timeliness. You can't improve what you can't measure in time to change the outcome.

## Medicare Star Ratings Leaders

### Mike Wilson, MBA — Director, Medicare Stars & Quality, CalOptima Health

**Context:** Nearly 10 years in managed care focused on risk adjustment and Stars for MA plans. CalOptima is a major public MA plan serving Medi-Cal and MA populations.

**Comment 1 (Stars/risk adjustment):**
The intersection of risk adjustment and quality measurement is where most plans lose efficiency. Accurate risk adjustment requires the same clinical data that drives HEDIS performance, but most organizations run these as separate data operations with separate teams. The plans consolidating those pipelines are seeing lift on both sides.

**Comment 2 (Public plan challenges):**
Public plans face a unique version of this problem because the member population is more transient and the clinical data is more fragmented across safety-net providers. The Stars methodology doesn't fully account for how much harder it is to close gaps when your members are seeing 8 different community health centers.

### Kimber Bishop, RN, BSN — Executive Director, Quality Performance, Acentra Health

**Context:** Corporate accountability for HEDIS performance and CMS Medicare Star ratings. Develops strategies for government programs. Clinical background (RN).

**Comment 1 (Quality strategy):**
The clinical perspective on quality measurement is underrepresented in the Stars conversation. Nurses on the front line of quality improvement know that the bottleneck isn't lack of protocols or care standards. It's getting the right data to the right clinician at the right time. Most quality programs are still built around retrospective identification rather than real-time clinical decision support.

Government program quality requirements are converging in ways that create both opportunity and complexity. Medicaid managed care, MA Stars, and MIPS are increasingly using overlapping measure sets. Organizations that build measurement infrastructure once and apply it across programs will have a structural advantage over those maintaining separate quality engines for each line of business.

### Randy Strite — Government Programs / Medicare Stars Consultant, FluidEdge

**Context:** Managed 4 CMS contracts, built Stars projection models. Independent consultant advising payers on Stars strategy.

**Comment 1 (Stars projection):**
Stars projection models are only as good as the underlying data timeliness. Most projection tools I've seen use claims data with 3-6 month lag, which means they're projecting from a position that's already outdated. The plans getting the most value from projections are feeding in clinical encounter data alongside claims to narrow that lag.

**Comment 2 (Consulting perspective):**
One pattern I see across payer clients is overinvestment in member outreach technology and underinvestment in the data pipeline that feeds it. You can have the most sophisticated IVR, text message, and home visit programs, but if the gap identification is 4 months old, you're calling members about problems that may already be resolved or past the intervention window.

**Comment 3 (CMS methodology):**
The CMS methodology changes in recent years have made Stars more volatile and harder to project. Cut point shifts, disaster provisions, and measure retirements create uncertainty that rewards plans with flexible, real-time quality infrastructure over those with rigid annual measurement cycles.

### Sachin Jain, MD, MBA — President & CEO, SCAN Group & Health Plan

**Context:** CEO of major MA plan. Very active LinkedIn poster on healthcare quality, Medicare policy, health equity, plan operations.

**Comment 1 (Medicare policy):**
The policy conversation around quality measurement tends to focus on what we measure and how we score it. But the operational bottleneck for most plans isn't the measure definition. It's the data pipeline between a clinical encounter and an actionable quality signal. That infrastructure gap determines whether quality programs are proactive or archival.

**Comment 2 (Health equity + quality):**
The health equity dimension of quality measurement is essentially a data timeliness problem in disguise. Vulnerable populations are more likely to receive care across fragmented provider networks, which means their clinical data takes longer to aggregate. **Comment 3 (Plan operations):**
One of the underappreciated aspects of running an MA plan is that quality measurement determines financial viability through Stars, but the measurement infrastructure is often the last thing to get investment. Revenue cycle, network adequacy, and member acquisition get the capital. Quality measurement gets a reporting team and an annual HEDIS project. That sequencing is backwards.

**Comment 1 (MIPS reporting):**
The APP Plus expansion from 6 to 11 measures is going to catch a lot of ACOs off guard. Most practices barely adapted their workflows for the original measure set. **Comment 2 (Practice education):**
The education gap in MIPS isn't about the rules. Most practices understand the reporting requirements. The gap is in understanding that the measurement methodology is converging with MA Stars, which means provider-side quality reporting is about to require the same real-time clinical data capabilities that payers have been building for years. Practices need to start thinking about measurement infrastructure, not just annual reporting compliance.

## NCQA & Standards Leadership

### Vivek Garg, MD, MBA — President & CEO, NCQA

**Context:** Leads the organization that defines HEDIS measures. Posts about quality measurement evolution, digital quality, health equity standards.

**Comment 1 (Quality measurement evolution):**
The shift toward digital quality measures is the right direction. But the implementation challenge is significant: most provider organizations still generate quality data as a byproduct of claims submission, not clinical encounters. ECDS-only measures assume a data infrastructure that exists at large health systems but is sparse in community health settings. Bridging that gap will determine whether digital quality measurement reduces or increases health equity disparities.

**Comment 2 (HEDIS modernization):**
The HEDIS modernization roadmap addresses measurement methodology comprehensively. The piece that needs equal attention is measurement latency. Annual measurement cycles made sense when claims were the primary data source. With real-time clinical data feeds becoming technically feasible, the question is whether the measurement framework can evolve to reward organizations that identify and close gaps in real time rather than retrospectively.

### Margaret O'Kane — Founder, NCQA (retired end 2025)

**Context:** Created HEDIS. Still active on LinkedIn sharing perspectives on quality measurement evolution and legacy.

**Comment 1 (Quality measurement legacy):**
HEDIS fundamentally changed how the industry thinks about healthcare quality by making it measurable and comparable. The next frontier is making it timely. The same rigor that HEDIS brought to measurement accuracy needs to be applied to measurement speed. An accurate gap identified too late to intervene is documentation, not quality improvement.

**Comment 2 (NCQA legacy):**
What made HEDIS transformative wasn't just standardization. It was accountability. For the first time, plans couldn't claim quality without evidence. The next version of that accountability needs to include timeliness: not just whether a gap was identified, but whether it was identified fast enough to change the clinical outcome.

### Edward Yurcisin — CTO, NCQA

**Context:** Leads NCQA's technology strategy including digital HEDIS, FHIR integration, quality measure technology infrastructure.

**Comment 1 (Digital HEDIS/technology):**
The technical architecture for digital quality measurement is converging on FHIR and CQL as the standard stack. The challenge isn't the specification. It's the implementation at scale across thousands of provider organizations with different EHR configurations, data models, and technical capabilities. Standardizing the measure definition is necessary but not sufficient. The execution infrastructure needs to be equally standardized.

**Comment 2 (FHIR integration):**
The FHIR-based approach to quality measurement has an underappreciated advantage: it can collapse the distinction between data exchange and quality calculation. Instead of extracting data, transmitting it, and then evaluating measures in a separate system, the measure logic can execute where the data lives. That architectural shift has implications for both latency and data governance.

## Quality Measurement Vendors & Consultants

### Jay Ackerman — CEO, Reveleer

**Context:** Posts about AI + quality measurement + care gaps. Active thought leader on how technology transforms quality operations. Reveleer does risk adjustment and quality.

**Comment 1 (AI + quality):**
The AI application in quality measurement that excites me most isn't NLP for chart extraction, although that's valuable. It's using AI to predict which gaps are most likely to be closable given the member's current care trajectory. Prioritizing outreach by closability rather than just gap existence could fundamentally change intervention ROI.

**Comment 2 (Care gap closure):**
The care gap closure conversation often focuses on the outreach channel: calls, texts, home visits, provider alerts. But the biggest determinant of closure rates is the age of the gap at the time of outreach. A gap identified last week has 3-4x the closure probability of a gap identified last quarter. The channel matters less than the timing.

**Comment 3 (Quality + risk adjustment):**
The convergence of risk adjustment and quality measurement operations is long overdue. Both require the same underlying clinical data, both benefit from real-time identification, and both suffer from the same data latency problems. Organizations running these as integrated operations are seeing compound returns that siloed teams can't match.

The compliance dimension of quality measurement gets more complex every year. ### Russ Kite — Medicare Stars / Quality Programs Leader

**Context:** Stars & HEDIS specialist with deep payer-side experience. Focused on operational quality improvement.

**Comment 1 (Operational quality):**
The operational reality of Stars improvement is that most plans have exhausted the easy interventions. The members with straightforward, single-gap closure opportunities have been reached. What's left are complex, multi-gap members who require coordinated interventions across multiple providers. That coordination requires real-time data visibility that most quality programs don't have.

**Comment 2 (Payer operations):**
One pattern I've observed: plans that reorganized their quality operations from measure-centric teams to member-centric teams consistently outperform. When one team owns all gaps for a member population instead of separate teams owning individual measures, the coordination overhead drops and closure rates improve. But that model only works if the team has real-time visibility across all measures simultaneously.
