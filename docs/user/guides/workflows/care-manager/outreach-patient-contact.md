# Outreach & Patient Contact

Effective patient outreach is at the heart of care management. This guide covers strategies for reaching patients, documenting contact, and handling common outreach challenges.

## Quick Reference

| Contact Method | Best For | Time | Contact Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phone Call | Immediate needs, complex topics | 5-15 min | 40-60% |
| Voicemail | Initial outreach, schedule attempt | 1-2 min | N/A |
| Text Message | Appointment reminders, simple info | 1 min | 70-80% |
| Email | Detailed information, documents | 2-3 min | 30-50% |
| In-Person Visit | Complex issues, vulnerable populations | 20-60 min | 100% (if scheduled) |

## Patient Lookup and Contact Information

### Finding Patient Contact Details
1. Click patient name in your caseload
2. From patient detail page, look for **Contact Information** section
3. Shows:
   - **Primary Phone**: Usually cell or home
   - **Alternate Phone**: Secondary number (spouse, family)
   - **Email**: If patient has provided
   - **Best Time to Call**: Patient's preference
   - **Language**: Primary language spoken
   - **Contact Barriers**: Documentation of known issues
     - Unreliable phone number
     - Works during business hours
     - Hearing impaired
     - Recently changed phone

### Interpreting Contact Flags
Look for icons indicating special considerations:
- 📵 **Phone Often Unreachable**: Try alternate methods
- 🌙 **Available After 6 PM**: Schedule calls accordingly
- 🗣️ **Spanish Only**: Arrange interpreter if needed
- 📧 **Prefers Email**: Use email for primary contact
- 📱 **Text Only**: Use texting platform
- ⛔ **Do Not Call**: Check reason and respect wishes

## Making Effective Patient Calls

### Pre-Call Preparation (2-3 minutes)
Before dialing:
1. **Review Patient Chart**:
   - Recent vitals/lab results
   - Medications
   - Recent visits
   - Outstanding issues
   - Care plan goals
2. **Know the Reason**: Be clear why you're calling
   - Follow-up from visit?
   - New alert/issue?
   - Routine check-in?
   - Specific question?
3. **Prepare Your Notes**: Have talking points ready
   - Key questions to ask
   - Information to provide
   - Actions you might need to take
4. **Choose Right Time**: Respect patient's preferred call time

### Opening the Call
Start with clear identification and purpose:
1. **Greeting**: "Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] calling from [Organization]. Do you have a couple of minutes?"
2. **Purpose**: "I'm your care manager, and I wanted to check in with you about [specific reason]."
3. **Confirmation**: "Is this still a good time to talk?"

**Why This Approach**:
- Professional tone establishes credibility
- Clear purpose helps patient engage
- Checking timing shows respect
- Patients more likely to stay on call

### Conducting the Call

**Core Conversation Elements**:

1. **Opening/Relationship Building** (1 minute):
   - "How have you been doing?"
   - "How's your health been?"
   - Personal comment (if appropriate)
   - Builds rapport

2. **Main Purpose** (3-10 minutes):
   - Address the reason for call
   - Ask open-ended questions
   - Listen more than you talk
   - Ask clarifying questions
   - Take notes on what patient says

3. **Barriers Assessment** (2-3 minutes):
   - "Is there anything making it hard to..."
   - "What's getting in the way?"
   - "Do you have questions?"
   - Common barriers:
     - Cost/insurance
     - Transportation
     - Work schedule
     - Childcare
     - Understanding/health literacy
     - Mistrust/cultural

4. **Education/Information** (2-5 minutes):
   - Provide relevant information
   - Keep simple/clear language
   - Avoid medical jargon
   - Check understanding ("Does that make sense?")
   - Provide written info if possible

5. **Agreements/Action Items** (2-3 minutes):
   - "So what I'm hearing is..."
   - Clarify what patient will do
   - What you'll do
   - When to follow up
   - Get commitment ("Does that sound okay?")
   - Give your contact info

6. **Closing** (1 minute):
   - Confirm next steps
   - Thank you for their time
   - "I'll call you on [date] to see how it's going"

**Good Call = 10-15 minutes of focused conversation**

### Documentation After Call

**Immediate Documentation** (within 1 hour):
1. Click **Log Call** or **New Note** in patient chart
2. Select **Call** as note type
3. Complete:
   - **Date/Time**: When call occurred
   - **Contact Status**: "Reached patient", "Left message", "Wrong number", "Refused"
   - **Call Duration**: Approximate time (5 min, 10 min, etc.)
   - **Topics Discussed**: What you talked about
   - **Key Findings**: What patient told you
   - **Agreements**: What patient committed to
   - **Barriers Identified**: Issues preventing compliance
   - **Action Items**: What you'll do next
   - **Follow-up Date**: When you'll call again
4. Save documentation
5. Create follow-up tasks if needed

**Example Documentation**:
"Called patient regarding medication adherence. Patient states she's been taking BP meds regularly but sometimes forgets. Will use phone alarm reminder. Discussed benefits of consistent BP control. Agreed to call back in 2 weeks. Patient receptive. No barriers identified."

## Voicemail Best Practices

### When to Leave Voicemail
Leave a message when:
- Patient doesn't answer
- You need to reach them but aren't in crisis mode
- Patient expects call but unavailable now

### Effective Voicemail

**Include**:
1. Your name and organization
2. Reason for call (specific, not vague)
3. Your callback number
4. Best time to reach you
5. Deadline for return call (if time-sensitive)

**Example Good Voicemail**:
"Hi Sarah, this is Amanda from Community Health's care management team. I wanted to check in with you about your diabetes management and see how the new insulin pen is working. Can you give me a call back at 555-1234? I'm available until 5 PM today and then again tomorrow morning after 9. Thanks!"

**Avoid**:
- Demanding tone
- Vague purpose
- No callback number
- Threatening language
- Leaving protected health information

### Follow-up on Voicemails
1. Document that voicemail was left
2. Plan follow-up attempt in 2-3 days
3. Try different time of day
4. Consider alternative contact method
5. Document multiple attempts

## Text Message Outreach

### When to Use Text
Good for:
- Appointment reminders ("Your visit with Dr. Smith is tomorrow at 2 PM")
- Quick updates ("We received your test results")
- Simple information ("Refill your prescription")
- Confirming patient contact info ("Is this still your current number?")

### Text Message Guidelines
1. **Keep it brief**: 1-2 sentences
2. **One call to action**: "Please call us back at..."
3. **Be professional**: No slang or abbreviations
4. **Include clinic info**: Name and number
5. **Respect privacy**: No Protected Health Information without consent
6. **Timing**: Business hours usually (unless specified otherwise)

**Example Good Text**:
"Hi [Name], this is [Clinic] care management. Your lab results are back - great news! Please call us at 555-1234 to discuss. -[Your initials]"

## Email Communication

### When to Use Email
Good for:
- Providing detailed written information
- Sending educational materials/documents
- Documenting agreements
- Following up detailed conversations
- Reaching patients who prefer email

### Email Guidelines
1. **Professional tone**: Business email standard
2. **Clear subject line**: "Follow-up from Your Doctor Visit"
3. **Organize information**: Use bullets/sections
4. **Plain language**: Avoid medical jargon
5. **Include next steps**: What you need from them
6. **Signature**: Full name, contact info, title
7. **Security**: Use encrypted email for Protected Health Information

**Example Email**:
Subject: Follow-up from Your Visit with Dr. Smith

Dear [Name],

Thank you for meeting with Dr. Smith on [date]. She wanted me to follow up with you about [topic].

Key points from your visit:
- [Point 1]
- [Point 2]
- [Point 3]

What you can do:
1. [Action 1]
2. [Action 2]

Please let me know if you have questions. I can be reached at [phone] or via email.

Best regards,
[Your name], Care Manager

## Handling Difficult Outreach Situations

### Scenario 1: Patient Avoids Calls
**Situation**: Patient screens calls, doesn't return messages, seems to avoid contact

**Approach**:
1. **Try multiple methods**: Phone, email, text, mail
2. **Vary timing**: Try different times of day
3. **Direct approach**: "I've been trying to reach you. Is there a better time?"
4. **Assess barriers**:
   - Mistrust of healthcare?
   - Don't see need for care management?
   - Prefer not to be managed?
5. **Respect boundaries**: Some patients simply don't want care management
6. **Document efforts**: Note multiple contact attempts

**What NOT to do**: Don't persist if patient clearly doesn't want contact (after 3-4 documented attempts)

### Scenario 2: Patient Frequently Cancels Appointments
**Situation**: Patient schedules but repeatedly cancels

**Approach**:
1. **Understand barriers**:
   - Transportation?
   - Cost/copay?
   - Anxiety about care?
   - Forgot/misunderstood appointment?
2. **Remove barriers if possible**:
   - Arrange transportation
   - Discuss copay concerns
   - Offer support person to attend
   - Send appointment reminders
3. **Motivational approach**:
   - Explore what patient wants for their health
   - Connect care to patient's goals
   - Reduce shame/judgment
4. **Last resort**: Work with physician to determine if care coordination appropriate for this patient

### Scenario 3: Patient Hostile or Angry
**Situation**: Patient yells, uses profanity, or threatens during call

**Approach**:
1. **Stay calm**: Don't match patient's tone
2. **Acknowledge emotion**: "I hear that you're frustrated"
3. **Don't argue**: You won't win
4. **Listen**: Often patient just needs to be heard
5. **Set boundaries**: "I want to help, but I need you to speak respectfully"
6. **Offer callback**: "Let me give you some time and call back later"
7. **Document**: Note the interaction and why (relevant context)
8. **Debrief**: Talk to supervisor if needed
9. **Safety plan**: If threatening, involve security/leadership

**Remember**: Hostility often about patient's situation, not about you

### Scenario 4: Patient Doesn't Speak English
**Situation**: Patient primary language is not English

**Approach**:
1. **Identify language**: Check patient record
2. **Use interpreter**:
   - Schedule calls with interpreter available
   - Use phone interpreter service if in-person not available
   - Allow extra time
   - Speak to patient, not interpreter
   - Give interpreter context before call
3. **Written materials**: Ensure translated materials available
4. **Respect**: Always honor patient's preferred language
5. **Documentation**: Note language used and interpreter info

**Resources**: Most organizations have interpreter services available

## Improving Contact Rates

### Strategies to Reach More Patients
1. **Call at different times**: Evening calls for working patients
2. **Try multiple numbers**: Use alternate numbers on file
3. **Alternative contacts**: Try emergency contact if patient unavailable
4. **Multiple methods**: If phone doesn't work, try text/email
5. **Consistency**: Regular attempts more successful than sporadic

### Tracking Contact Patterns
1. Use **Call Log Reports** to analyze:
   - Which patients hardest to reach?
   - What times/methods most successful?
   - How many attempts before reach?
   - What barriers are patterns?
2. Adjust approach based on data
3. Share findings with team

## Outreach Success Metrics

### Track Your Performance
1. **Contact Rate**: % of patients you reach per attempt
2. **First-Contact Success**: % reached on first call
3. **Average Attempts**: How many tries before success
4. **Appointment Completion**: % of patients who keep scheduled appointments
5. **Patient Satisfaction**: How patients feel about outreach

### Benchmarking
- Typical contact rate: 40-60%
- Typical first-contact success: 30-40%
- Industry average attempts to reach: 2-3
