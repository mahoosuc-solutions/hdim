# Risk Assessment & Stratification

Risk stratification helps you identify which patients need the most intensive care management. This guide explains how to understand and use risk scores.

## Understanding Risk Scores

### What Is a Risk Score?
A risk score is a number (usually 0-100) that predicts:
- Likelihood of hospitalization in next 12 months
- Likelihood of high healthcare costs
- Likelihood of poor health outcomes
- Need for intensive care management

Higher score = higher risk = more intensive management needed

### How Scores Are Calculated
Risk scores combine:
1. **Clinical Data** (40%):
   - Diagnoses (number and severity)
   - Recent hospitalizations/ED visits
   - Lab values
   - Medications
   - Functional status
2. **Utilization Patterns** (40%):
   - Number of ED visits
   - Hospital admissions
   - Frequent office visits
   - Multiple providers seen
3. **Social Factors** (20%):
   - Age
   - Housing stability
   - Social support
   - Health literacy
   - Barriers to care

### Why It Matters
- **For Patients**: Higher-risk patients benefit most from intensive care management
- **For Providers**: Helps allocate limited resources to those needing most help
- **For Organizations**: Predicts healthcare costs and identifies intervention opportunities
- **For Outcomes**: Early intervention can prevent crises

## Risk Tiers

### Red (Critical Risk)
**Score**: Typically 75-100
**Characteristics**:
- Recent hospitalization (within 3 months)
- Multiple ED visits (3+ per year)
- Severe mental illness or substance use
- Complex medical conditions
- Very high healthcare costs predicted

**Your Role**:
- Contact weekly or more frequently
- Intensive care coordination
- Address immediate safety needs
- Prevent ED/hospital readmission
- Connect to intensive services (case management, psychiatry, etc.)
- Expected caseload: 5-10 patients per care manager

### Orange (High Risk)
**Score**: Typically 50-74
**Characteristics**:
- Uncontrolled chronic disease
- Recent hospitalization (within 6 months)
- One or more ED visits in past year
- Social barriers to care
- Medication non-compliance

**Your Role**:
- Contact biweekly or monthly
- Regular monitoring
- Care plan with clear goals
- Address barriers proactively
- Coordinate with providers
- Expected caseload: 15-25 patients per care manager

### Yellow (Moderate Risk)
**Score**: Typically 25-49
**Characteristics**:
- Stable chronic disease
- No recent hospitalization
- Preventive care gaps
- Some social barriers
- Reasonable health literacy

**Your Role**:
- Contact monthly to quarterly
- Standard care plan
- Preventive focus
- Patient education
- Routine monitoring
- Expected caseload: 30-50 patients per care manager

### Green (Low Risk)
**Score**: Typically 0-24
**Characteristics**:
- Healthy or very stable conditions
- No healthcare utilization spikes
- Strong social support
- Good self-management
- Engaged with care

**Your Role**:
- Minimal intervention needed
- Likely transitioning out of care management
- Maintenance contact annually
- Support for any new issues
- Expected caseload: 20-30 patients per care manager

## Viewing Risk Information

### Dashboard Risk Widget
1. From home dashboard, look for **Patient Risk Summary**
2. Shows:
   - How many red, orange, yellow, green patients you have
   - Visual breakdown (pie chart)
   - Highest-risk patients listed
   - Average risk score of your caseload

### Patient Detail Risk Card
1. Click patient name
2. Look for **Risk Assessment** card
3. Shows:
   - **Overall Risk Score** (0-100)
   - **Risk Tier** (Red/Orange/Yellow/Green)
   - **Primary Risk Factors** (top 3)
   - **Score Trend** (improving/declining)
   - **Last Updated** date

### Risk Factors Details
Click **View Full Risk Assessment**:
1. Shows all risk factors contributing to score
2. Lists factors in order of impact:
   - Which factors most important?
   - Which could be addressed?
3. Shows:
   - **Potentially Modifiable** (you can work on these)
   - **Non-Modifiable** (age, some conditions)
4. Shows **Recommended Interventions**

### Risk Trending
1. From risk card, click **View Trend**
2. Shows 6-12 month history of risk score
3. Helps identify:
   - Is patient improving or declining?
   - Did your interventions work?
   - When did risk change significantly?
   - What was the trigger?

## Using Risk for Care Planning

### Risk-Based Prioritization
Allocate your time based on risk tier:

**Monday Morning** (1 hour):
1. Filter caseload to **Red tier only**
2. Plan week's interactions for each
3. Determine contact frequency/intensity
4. Identify barriers and needed resources

**Then allocate remaining time**:
- 40% of time → Red patients
- 35% of time → Orange patients
- 15% of time → Yellow patients
- 10% of time → Green patients + admin

### Developing Risk-Appropriate Care Plans
Match care intensity to risk tier:

**Red Patient Plan**:
- Frequent contact (weekly+)
- Multiple interventions per goal
- Intensive team coordination
- Focus on preventing hospitalization
- May include in-home visits
- Focus on immediate safety

**Orange Patient Plan**:
- Regular contact (biweekly-monthly)
- Standard interventions
- Routine coordination
- Proactive problem-solving
- Office or phone visits
- Focus on disease management

**Yellow Patient Plan**:
- Regular contact (monthly-quarterly)
- Standard interventions
- Routine monitoring
- Preventive focus
- Phone or minimal office contact
- Education-focused

**Green Patient Plan**:
- Minimal contact (PRN or annual)
- May not need formal plan
- Self-management support
- Phone contact only
- Graduation from program likely

## Identifying High-Impact Interventions

### What Drives Risk?
Understanding what factors contribute most to patient's risk helps prioritize interventions.

**Example Red Patient**:
- Score 85
- Top risk factors: Recent hospitalization (35 points), heart failure (25 points), depression untreated (15 points), lives alone (10 points)

**High-Impact Interventions** (order by potential impact):
1. **Prevent re-hospitalization** (biggest opportunity; could reduce 35 points)
   - Close monitoring
   - Medication compliance
   - Early warning signs education
   - Provider coordination

2. **Optimize heart failure care** (25 points potential)
   - Medication optimization
   - Fluid monitoring
   - Diet management
   - Cardiology coordination

3. **Address depression** (15 points potential)
   - Screen formally (PHQ-9)
   - Refer to mental health
   - Support treatment engagement
   - Regular check-ins

4. **Address social isolation** (10 points potential)
   - Connect to community resources
   - Support system development
   - Consider assisted living/services
   - Facilitate social engagement

### Focusing on Modifiable Factors
Not all risk factors can change:
- **Can't change**: Age, past medical history, genetics
- **Can change**: Medication adherence, control of disease, addressing depression, housing stability, social support

Focus your effort on modifiable factors with highest impact.

## Risk Communication with Patients

### Discussing Risk with Patient
Be careful with risk terminology (can be scary):

**How to Frame It**:
1. Focus on "opportunity," not "danger"
   - NOT: "Your risk score is very high"
   - YES: "I can help you with several areas that would really improve your health"
2. Use understandable language
   - NOT: "Your COPD exacerbation risk is elevated"
   - YES: "Your breathing problems could get worse. Let's work on preventing that"
3. Focus on modifiable factors
   - NOT: "Because you're 72 and diabetic..."
   - YES: "Working on your blood sugar control and blood pressure could really help"
4. Offer hope and partnership
   - "I believe we can make real progress on this together"
   - "Let's focus on the areas where you can make the biggest difference"

### Shared Decision-Making
1. Explain your observations
2. Get patient input:
   - What areas matter most to patient?
   - What are patient's goals?
   - What can patient realistically commit to?
3. Build plan together
4. Revisit and adjust based on progress

## Monitoring Risk Changes

### Risk Score Updates
Risk scores typically update:
- **Monthly**: New data incorporated
- **Quarterly**: Comprehensive updates
- **After major events**: Hospital admission, ED visit, new diagnosis

When you see risk change:
1. Check what triggered it:
   - New hospitalization?
   - Medication change?
   - New diagnosis?
   - Utilization spike?
2. Determine if expected or concerning
3. Adjust care plan if needed

### Declining Risk - Celebrate!
When patient's risk score improves:
1. Identify what worked:
   - Which interventions helped?
   - What did patient do differently?
   - What changed in their life?
2. Share success with patient
   - "Your blood sugar control improved!"
   - "You haven't been to the ED in 6 months!"
3. Reinforce what's working
4. Consider graduation from intensive management

### Increasing Risk - Investigate Quickly
When risk increases unexpectedly:
1. Contact patient immediately
2. Assess what happened:
   - New medical issue?
   - Medication compliance?
   - New stressor?
   - Job/housing loss?
3. Intensify interventions if needed
4. Adjust care plan
5. Involve physician/team

## Risk Stratification for Populations

### Your Caseload Distribution
Healthy caseload should show:
- **Red**: 5-15% (very high-need)
- **Orange**: 25-35% (high-need)
- **Yellow**: 40-50% (moderate-need)
- **Green**: 10-20% (low-need)

If heavily skewed toward red, you're managing highest-risk patients (intense work). If heavily green, more maintenance focus.

### Panel Health Trends
Monitor your panel's overall health:
1. From **Population Health**, view risk distribution
2. Is your panel's average risk improving or worsening?
3. Any new high-risk patients added?
4. Success rate moving patients down risk tiers?

## Best Practices

### Risk Assessment Excellence
1. ✅ Understand what drives each patient's risk
2. ✅ Focus on modifiable factors
3. ✅ Match interventions to risk tier
4. ✅ Monitor and adjust frequently
5. ✅ Celebrate improvements
6. ✅ Escalate worsening quickly

### Avoid Common Mistakes
1. ❌ Ignoring risk scores (they have data)
2. ❌ Treating all patients same intensity (wasted resources)
3. ❌ Focusing only on non-modifiable factors
4. ❌ Not addressing social determinants
5. ❌ Forgetting to celebrate success
