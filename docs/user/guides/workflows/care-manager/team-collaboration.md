# Team Collaboration

Effective patient outcomes depend on coordinated care across your entire team. This guide covers communicating and collaborating with physicians, nurses, and other care team members.

## Your Care Team

### Typical Team Structure
**For Each Patient**:
1. **Primary Care Physician**: Overall medical care
2. **Care Manager (You)**: Care coordination, outreach
3. **Nurse or RN**: Clinical assessment, patient education
4. **Specialist Physicians**: For specific conditions (cardiologist, endocrinologist, etc.)
5. **Behavioral Health**: Mental health, substance use support
6. **Pharmacy**: Medication management
7. **Social Work**: Social services, community resources
8. **Administrator**: Scheduling, insurance, administrative support

**Your Role**: Coordinator who ensures all parts work together

### Your Team Contacts
1. Click **Team Directory** or **My Contacts**
2. Find phone/email for all team members
3. Your supervising nurse or physician
4. Secondary contacts if primary unavailable
5. Save frequently used numbers

## Communication Methods

### Secure Messaging
Best for: Non-urgent coordination, details, documentation

**How to Use**:
1. From patient detail, click **Care Team Messages**
2. Click **New Message**
3. Select recipient (physician, nurse, etc.)
4. Type your message:
   - Be specific about issue
   - Include relevant data
   - Ask clear question or make clear request
   - Example: "Please review this patient's A1C (8.2%). Are we increasing metformin as discussed?"
5. Click **Send**
6. Recipient gets notification and can reply in system
7. All messages saved in patient chart

**Advantages**:
- Documented in chart
- Can include attachments
- Asynchronous (don't need immediate response)
- Time-stamped

**Disadvantages**:
- Delayed response
- Not for urgent issues
- Can be overused (volume)

### Direct Phone Calls
Best for: Urgent issues, complex discussions, relationship-building

**When to Call**:
- Urgent patient safety issue
- Patient in crisis
- Complex medical question needing discussion
- Important decision to make together
- To introduce yourself to new team member

**Making the Call**:
1. **Prepare**: Know what you're calling about
2. **Timing**: Respect their schedule ("Do you have 5 minutes?")
3. **Clear reason**: "I'm calling about [patient] who has [issue]. I need [advice/action]."
4. **Listen**: Don't just download; listen to their perspective
5. **Next steps**: Clarify what will happen next
6. **Document**: After call, log it in patient chart briefly

**Example**: "Hi Dr. Smith, I'm calling about Maria Garcia. She called me about severe shortness of breath after walking yesterday. I'm concerned this might be heart failure exacerbation. Can you see her today? What should I tell her to do right now?"

### Task Assignments
Best for: Action-oriented items, clear deliverables

**Create a Task**:
1. From patient detail, click **Team Tasks** or **New Task**
2. Fill in:
   - **Task**: What needs to be done
   - **Assigned To**: Which team member
   - **Due Date**: When needed
   - **Priority**: Urgent/High/Medium/Low
   - **Details**: Specific information
3. Click **Assign**
4. Team member gets notification
5. Can track completion status

**Examples**:
- "Please refill patient's metformin (due date: today)"
- "Schedule follow-up appointment for [date]"
- "Call patient about medication side effects"

### Patient Care Conferences
Best for: Complex patients, multiple providers, care plan decisions

**Scheduling a Conference**:
1. From patient detail, click **Request Care Conference**
2. Select participants:
   - Primary physician
   - Nurses
   - Specialist if relevant
   - Social work if relevant
   - Other providers
3. Suggest times (system shows availability)
4. Provide agenda items:
   - Patient's current status
   - Barriers identified
   - Proposed plan changes
   - Coordination needs
5. Send invites

**During Conference**:
- Ensure all providers share their perspective
- Align on shared care plan
- Clarify responsibilities
- Agree on timeline
- Document decisions

**After Conference**:
- Distribute care plan updates
- Ensure all parties understand decisions
- Add follow-up tasks

## Coordinating Care for Complex Patients

### Multi-Specialist Coordination
When patient sees multiple specialists:

1. **Centralize Information**: You are information hub
   - Get summaries from each specialist
   - Share relevant information across providers
   - Prevent duplication
   - Catch contradictions

2. **Medication Reconciliation**: Ensure consistent medications
   - Each specialist may prescribe
   - You coordinate to ensure no duplicates or interactions
   - Flag concerns to primary physician
   - Patient takes one consolidated med list

3. **Test Coordination**: Prevent unnecessary duplicate testing
   - Check if test already ordered elsewhere
   - Share results across team
   - Avoid repeating expensive/uncomfortable tests
   - Ensure all providers aware of results

4. **Plan Alignment**: Ensure all providers working toward same goals
   - Cardiologist focused on heart
   - Endocrinologist focused on diabetes
   - You focused on overall health and goals
   - Physician coordinates when specialists disagree

### Managing Conflicting Recommendations
Sometimes specialists recommend different things:

**Example**:
- Cardiologist wants aggressive fluid restriction for heart failure
- Nephrologist wants less fluid restriction due to kidney function
- What does patient actually do?

**Your Role**:
1. **Identify conflict**: Notice when recommendations differ
2. **Gather information**: Understand why each specialist recommends what
3. **Ask questions**: "Help me understand why..." to each provider
4. **Facilitate discussion**: Can specialists talk to each other?
5. **Escalate if needed**: Involve primary physician to make final call
6. **Communicate clearly to patient**: What will patient actually do?
7. **Monitor**: Does approach work or cause problems?

## Documentation of Coordination

### Care Team Notes
Document your team interactions:

1. From **Care Team Messages** section, view message history
   - All messages auto-documented in chart
   - Shows who said what and when
   - Chronological record of coordination

2. For phone calls, create brief **Care Coordination Note**:
   - Date/time of call
   - Who you spoke with
   - Issue discussed
   - Recommendations/next steps
   - Example: "Called Dr. Smith 2/15 at 2 PM regarding A1C. Recommended increasing metformin dose to 1000 mg BID. Patient agrees."

3. For conferences, attach **Conference Summary**:
   - Who attended
   - Patient status discussed
   - Decisions made
   - Plan revisions
   - Follow-up items

### Clear Communication in Writing
When documenting or messaging:

**Be Specific**:
- ❌ "Patient not doing well"
- ✅ "Patient's A1C 8.2%, up from 7.1% three months ago. Taking metformin 500 mg BID. States taking all doses."

**Ask Clear Questions**:
- ❌ "What do you think about the diabetes?"
- ✅ "Should we increase metformin dose given A1C trend, or consider adding another agent?"

**Provide Context**:
- ❌ "Patient missed appointment"
- ✅ "Patient works until 6 PM. Appointment was at 2 PM. Can we schedule after hours?"

**Make Actionable Requests**:
- ❌ "Can you do something about medications?"
- ✅ "Can you renew patient's lisinopril and consider adding amlodipine for BP control? BP running 145-155 systolic."

## Building Relationships with Team Members

### First Introductions
When starting work with new provider:

1. **Introduce yourself**: "Hi Dr. ____, I'm ____, the care manager for your patients with us."
2. **Explain your role**: "I help coordinate care and improve patient outcomes by managing the care plan."
3. **Ask preferences**: "What's the best way to reach you with questions?" "Do you prefer email, messaging, or calls?"
4. **Show respect**: "I'm new to this. I'll learn your preferences and work style."
5. **Offer support**: "I'm here to help make your job easier."

### Building Trust
Trust develops over time through:
1. **Reliability**: Do what you say
2. **Competence**: Know your stuff
3. **Respect**: Value their expertise
4. **Communication**: Keep them informed
5. **Initiative**: Make their life easier (anticipate needs)
6. **Humility**: Admit when you don't know
7. **Follow-through**: Document agreements and complete tasks

### Working with Difficult Team Members
Sometimes colleagues are challenging:

**If Physician Seems Dismissive of Care Management**:
1. Don't take it personally
2. Focus on helping patients (not proving your worth)
3. Build relationship over time through good work
4. Invite collaboration ("Let's work together on this patient")
5. Escalate if concerns about patient care

**If Colleague Seems Overwhelmed**:
1. Recognize they may be busy
2. Make requests easy ("I can follow up on this if you want")
3. Respect their time
4. Offer support where appropriate
5. Give them credit for good work

**If There's Genuine Disagreement**:
1. Understand their perspective fully
2. Share your perspective clearly
3. Seek common ground (patient's benefit)
4. If can't resolve, escalate to supervisor
5. Support final decision made

## Troubleshooting Collaboration Issues

### "Physician Not Responding to Messages"
**Approach**:
1. Don't keep repeating same message
2. Call directly for urgent issues
3. Use clear subject lines (patient name, specific issue)
4. Ask their preference for how to reach them
5. Understand they may be very busy

### "Team Members Giving Conflicting Advice"
**Approach**:
1. Recognize both perspectives have merit
2. Ask questions to understand rationale
3. Facilitate conversation between them if possible
4. Involve primary physician if major disagreement
5. Document discussion and final decision clearly

### "Can't Reach a Specialist About Patient"
**Approach**:
1. Try different contact methods
2. Reach out to specialist's care coordinator
3. Follow up through electronic system
4. Escalate through primary physician
5. Document your attempts

## Collaboration Best Practices

### Excellence in Teamwork
✅ **DO**:
- Communicate clearly and specifically
- Respect everyone's expertise
- Follow up on commitments
- Share information appropriately
- Celebrate team successes
- Ask for help when needed
- Give credit to others
- Address issues promptly

❌ **DON'T**:
- Bypass chain of command unnecessarily
- Blame colleagues privately
- Repeat yourself endlessly
- Make demands without context
- Take things personally
- Document conflicts in patient chart
- Gossip about team members
- Ignore feedback
