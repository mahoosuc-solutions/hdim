# Patient Communication

Clear, compassionate communication improves patient outcomes and satisfaction. This guide covers communication methods available through the platform.

## Communication Channels

### Secure Messaging (Most Common)
**When to Use**: Non-urgent clinical questions, test results, appointment reminders, follow-up information

**How to Send**:
1. From patient detail page, click **Send Message**
2. Choose **Patient Portal Message**
3. Type your message:
   - Keep it clear and understandable
   - Avoid medical jargon when possible
   - Organize with line breaks
   - Spell out abbreviations first time
4. Click **Send**
5. Patient receives notification via patient portal or email

**Best Practices**:
- ✅ Respond within 24 business hours
- ✅ Use clear subject lines
- ✅ Organize with bullets or short paragraphs
- ✅ Include specific next steps
- ✅ Be warm and professional

**Example**:
"Hi Maria, Your lab results are back! Good news - your A1C is 6.8%, which is right in goal. Keep up with your current plan. See you at your appointment next Thursday at 2 PM. If questions before then, just reply here."

### Phone Calls
**When to Use**: Urgent issues, complex discussions, patient prefers phone, difficult conversations

**Best Practices**:
- ✅ Call at appropriate times (respect patient privacy)
- ✅ Introduce yourself clearly
- ✅ State reason for call
- ✅ Listen more than you talk
- ✅ Use layperson's language
- ✅ Ask patient to repeat back understanding
- ✅ Document call in patient record

**Documentation**: After call, create call note in patient chart

### Video Visits/Telehealth
**When to Use**: Routine follow-ups, chronic disease management, convenience for patient

**Setup**:
1. Schedule telehealth appointment
2. Send patient link to video conference
3. Confirm patient technology works (internet, camera)
4. Start on time
5. Document encounter same as office visit

### In-Person Visits
**When to Use**: Physical exam needed, complex/serious issues, first visit with patient, patient prefers in-person

**Preparation**:
1. Review chart before visit
2. Plan what to cover
3. Prepare education materials if relevant
4. Arrange quiet space for conversation
5. Minimize interruptions

### Group Education/Classes
**When to Use**: Common conditions (diabetes, hypertension), prevention topics, lifestyle changes

**Examples Available in System**:
- Diabetes self-management classes
- Blood pressure management classes
- Heart disease prevention
- Weight management
- Smoking cessation
- Mental health support groups

**How to Refer Patient**:
1. From patient detail, click **Refer to Class**
2. Select appropriate class
3. Send referral to patient
4. System tracks attendance
5. Document that patient was referred

## Communicating Test Results

### Positive Results (Normal/Good)
**Best Practice**: Share promptly with positive framing

**Message Template**:
"Good news! Your [test] came back normal/in goal. This shows your [condition management] is working well. Keep doing what you're doing. See you at your next appointment on [date]."

### Abnormal Results
**Best Practice**: Call patient first (don't just message); follow with written summary

**Process**:
1. **Call patient** with results
   - Explain findings in understandable language
   - Discuss what it means
   - Explain next steps
   - Answer questions
   - Show empathy if concerning
2. **Follow up with message** confirming discussion
   - What was discussed
   - What patient needs to do
   - When to follow up
   - Emergency symptoms to watch for

**Example Call**: "Hi John, I wanted to reach you about your recent lab results. Your potassium came back a little low at 3.2. This isn't urgent, but we want to bring it up a bit. I'm changing your medication to help with that. I'll need to recheck it in 2 weeks. Any questions?"

**Example Follow-up Message**: "Hi John, Following up on our phone conversation about your labs - we're adjusting your medication to help normalize your potassium. Important: If you experience unusual weakness or heart palpitations, go to the ER. Otherwise, come see me in 2 weeks for recheck. Let me know if you have questions."

### Concerning Results
**Best Practice**: Urgent communication and follow-up

**Process**:
1. **Immediate communication** (within hours):
   - Call patient right away
   - Be direct but not alarming
   - Explain significance
   - State clearly what to do
   - Offer support
2. **Determine urgency**:
   - Can wait for scheduled appointment? (unlikely)
   - Needs urgent appointment? (likely)
   - Needs emergency care? (possibly)
3. **Facilitate next steps**:
   - If urgent: make appointment within days
   - If emergency: direct to ER
   - If moderate: schedule within week
4. **Follow up**:
   - Call after appointment
   - Ensure treatment started
   - Monitor progress

## Patient Education

### When to Provide Education
- **New diagnosis**: Patient needs to understand condition
- **New medication**: Patient needs to know how to take it
- **Lifestyle change**: Patient needs behavior change support
- **Test preparation**: Patient needs to prepare for upcoming test
- **Procedure**: Patient needs informed consent and preparation

### Education Methods

**1. Verbal Education** (During Visit)
- Discuss in simple terms
- Use pictures/diagrams if helpful
- Ask questions to check understanding
- Provide written handout to take home
- Document: "Discussed [topic] with patient. Patient demonstrated understanding."

**2. Written Materials**
Available in system:
- Pre-made condition-specific handouts
- Medication information
- Lifestyle change guides
- Preparation instructions
- Disease management resources

**How to Share**:
1. From patient detail, click **Patient Education**
2. Search for relevant material
3. Review before sharing (ensure accuracy)
4. Send to patient via message or print
5. Document: "Provided written education on [topic]"

**3. Video Education**
System may have videos on:
- Disease management
- Medication use
- Lifestyle changes
- Preparation instructions
- Patient stories/testimonials

Send links to patient if appropriate.

**4. Classes and Support Groups**
Refer to available classes:
- Diabetes self-management
- Heart disease prevention
- Mental health support
- Chronic pain management
- Smoking cessation

## Shared Decision-Making

### What Is SDM?
Shared Decision-Making is partnership approach where:
1. You (provider) present clinical evidence and options
2. Patient shares preferences and values
3. Together you decide on plan respecting both perspectives

### SDM Process

**Step 1: Explore Patient Values**
- "What's most important to you about your health?"
- "What are you willing to do?"
- "What concerns do you have about treatment?"
- "What does success look like to you?"

**Step 2: Present Options**
- "We have a few options. Let me explain each one."
- Present evidence for each option
- Discuss pros/cons
- Be honest about uncertainty
- Avoid being directive ("You should...")

**Step 3: Support Decision**
- "What sounds best to you?"
- "Do you have any other questions?"
- "Let's try this approach. If it doesn't work, we can revisit."

**Step 4: Document**
"Discussed [topic] with patient. Reviewed options: [option A], [option B], [option C]. Patient chose [option] because [patient's rationale]. Plan: [what you'll do]."

### Example: Blood Pressure Medication

**Your Exploration**:
"Your blood pressure has been running high. We could treat this a few ways. Before I tell you about them, what are your concerns? Are there side effects that worry you?"

**Your Options**:
"We have three options: 1) Start medication today, 2) Try diet/exercise changes for 3 months first, or 3) Start low-dose medication plus lifestyle changes. Here's what research shows about each..."

**Pros/Cons**:
- Medication: Works faster, but possible side effects
- Lifestyle: No medication side effects, but takes time and effort
- Combination: Often best result, but needs commitment

**Patient Decision**:
"I'll try the combination approach. I'm willing to work on diet and exercise."

**Your Documentation**:
"Discussed BP management options with patient. Reviewed medication vs. lifestyle vs. combination approach. Patient prefers to start lifestyle modifications plus low-dose lisinopril. Agrees to diet/exercise changes. Will monitor BP and assess in 6 weeks."

## Difficult Conversations

### Delivering Bad News
**Framework (SPIKES)**:
1. **Setting**: Private, quiet, no interruptions
2. **Perception**: Ask what patient already knows
3. **Invitation**: Ask what patient wants to know
4. **Knowledge**: Give information clearly, compassionately
5. **Emotions**: Acknowledge feelings, offer support
6. **Summary/Strategy**: Summarize and plan next steps

**Example**: New cancer diagnosis
- Setting: Private office
- Perception: "What have you heard so far?"
- Invitation: "Are you ready to hear more about the results?"
- Knowledge: "Your biopsy shows cancer. I know this is shocking. We have treatment options."
- Emotions: "This is hard news. It's normal to feel overwhelmed. We're going to help you through this."
- Strategy: "Here's what happens next. We'll see an oncologist. Let me help coordinate that."

### Addressing Non-Compliance
**Approach** (Don't blame; understand barriers):
1. "I notice you haven't started the medication yet. Tell me what's getting in the way?"
2. Listen without judgment
3. Problem-solve barriers
4. Offer support
5. Reset expectations
6. Follow up

**Example**:
- "You've missed three appointments. I'm concerned. What's happening?"
- "Work schedule shifted. Hard to get time off."
- "I understand. Let's find a time that works better. Would evening or Saturday morning help?"
- "Saturday morning would work."
- "Great. I'll have our scheduler find you a Saturday slot. Let's also have a backup plan for transportation."

## Documentation of Communication

### Message History
All messages automatically saved in patient record:
- Visible in **Message Center** or **Care Team Messages**
- Time-stamped
- Shows who sent what to whom
- Searchable

### Phone Call Documentation
After phone calls, create note:
1. Click **Add Note**
2. Select **Phone Call**
3. Document:
   - Date/time of call
   - Reason
   - What was discussed
   - Patient's response
   - Actions taken/agreed
   - Follow-up plan

### Education Documentation
When educating patient, document:
"Provided [type of] education regarding [topic]. Patient verbalized understanding/demonstrated [skill]. Questions answered. Patient given [handout/referral/etc.]."

## Confidentiality and Security

### HIPAA Compliance
- ✅ Use secure messaging (encrypted)
- ✅ Don't discuss via regular email
- ✅ Don't text using personal phone
- ✅ Don't leave protected info on voicemail
- ✅ Ensure patient portal is private

### Patient Privacy
- ✅ Don't discuss patient with others except care team
- ✅ Don't share patient information with family without permission
- ✅ Don't post about patients on social media (ever!)
- ✅ Maintain professional boundaries

## Patient Portal Access

### What Patients Can See
Patients can access through patient portal:
- Recent visit summaries
- Current medications
- Test results
- Educational materials
- Appointment history
- Secure messaging

### What Patients Cannot See
- Provider notes (may be hidden per policy)
- Internal team communications
- Billing details (sometimes)
- Mental health notes (sometimes protected)

Verify what patients can see in your system.

## Best Practices

### Communication Excellence
✅ **DO**:
- Be clear and use simple language
- Respect patient preferences for communication
- Document conversations
- Respond timely to messages
- Use empathy and compassion
- Verify understanding
- Offer support resources
- Maintain confidentiality

❌ **DON'T**:
- Use medical jargon without explanation
- Communicate urgent results via message
- Make decisions without patient input
- Share information without consent
- Discuss patients publicly
- Use insecure communication methods
- Assume patient understands
- Dismiss patient concerns
