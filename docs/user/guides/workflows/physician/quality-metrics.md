# Reviewing Quality Metrics

Quality metrics measure how well you and your team are delivering evidence-based care. This guide explains what the metrics mean, how to interpret them, and how to use them to improve patient outcomes.

## Quick Reference

| Metric Category | Common Examples | Key Focus | Improvement Method |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Preventive Care** | Screenings, vaccinations | % of eligible patients up-to-date | Complete services; prevent gaps |
| **Chronic Disease** | Monitoring, control rates | % with adequate management | Intensify treatment; close gaps |
| **Medication Safety** | Appropriate prescribing | % on recommended medications | Review protocols; update meds |
| **Mental Health** | Depression screening | % screened annually | Add screenings; document |
| **Care Coordination** | Follow-up completion | % with timely follow-up | Improve outreach; schedule visits |
| **Patient Experience** | Satisfaction, access | How patients rate care | Focus on communication; reduce wait times |

## Quality Metrics Overview

### What Are Quality Metrics?
Quality metrics are standardized measurements of clinical performance based on evidence-based guidelines. They track:
- **Whether recommended care is delivered**: Do patients get needed services?
- **Whether services are timely**: Are services provided when needed?
- **Whether care is effective**: Do services improve patient outcomes?

### Why They Matter
1. **Patient Care**: Ensure you're following best practices
2. **Outcomes**: Measure whether care actually improves health
3. **Performance Payment**: Many payment models tie reimbursement to metric performance
4. **Accountability**: Document that care meets standards
5. **Improvement**: Identify where to focus quality improvement efforts

### Common Metric Types

**Process Metrics**:
- Did you do the service? (e.g., "Percentage of diabetic patients who got A1C test")
- Most common metrics in quality programs
- Directly affected by your care gap closures

**Outcome Metrics**:
- Did the patient improve? (e.g., "Percentage of diabetic patients with A1C <7%")
- Affected by care quality, patient adherence, treatment effectiveness
- Harder to influence but more clinically meaningful

**Experience Metrics**:
- How did the patient experience care? (e.g., Patient satisfaction scores)
- Affected by communication, access, coordination
- Increasingly important in quality measurement

## Understanding Individual Metrics

### Metric Display Components

Each metric typically shows:

**Numerator/Denominator**:
- Example: "85/100" means 85 patients out of 100 eligible
- Numerator: Number achieving metric
- Denominator: Total eligible patients
- Percentage: 85%

**Benchmark Comparison**:
- Your performance vs. standard
- Example: "Your: 85% | Benchmark: 90%"
- "You are 5 percentage points below benchmark"
- Shows target to achieve

**Trend Chart**:
- Your performance over time (past 12 months)
- Shows improvement/decline trajectory
- Helps identify if changes you made are working

**Actionable Insights**:
- System analyzes your data
- Suggests actions to improve metric
- Examples:
  - "You have 12 diabetic patients without recent A1C test. Close these gaps to improve metric by 12 percentage points"
  - "Your closure time is 30 days; benchmark is 14 days. Focus on faster response"

### Reading a Specific Metric

**Example: Hemoglobin A1C Control in Diabetics**

"Percentage of patients 18-75 years with type 2 diabetes who have most recent A1C test result <7%"

**What This Measures**:
- Captures two things: (1) whether A1C test was done, and (2) whether result was good
- Patients either achieve or don't achieve this metric
- A patient with A1C 7.2% doesn't meet metric (even though close to goal)

**Denominators**:
- All patients in your panel with type 2 diabetes
- Age 18-75 (excludes very young and very old)
- Must have no contraindications to tight control
- Patients with type 1 diabetes usually excluded (different guidelines)

**Why It Matters**:
- Tight glycemic control prevents complications
- Reduces risk of heart disease, kidney disease, neuropathy, eye disease
- Directly impacts patient outcomes

**How To Improve**:
1. Close A1C testing gaps (get all diabetics tested within 6 months)
2. Intensify therapy for patients with A1C >7%
3. Check medication adherence
4. Provide patient education on importance
5. Coordinate with endocrinology for complex cases

**Your Numbers** (example):
- **Your Performance**: 82%
- **Benchmark**: 90%
- **Gap**: 8 percentage points (9 patients)
- **To Improve**: Close 9 patients' A1C control (get test done or adjust meds)

## Common HEDIS and Quality Measures

HealthData in Motion tracks multiple quality measure sets. Here are commonly tracked measures:

### Diabetes Management (DBE)
**Measures**:
1. **A1C Testing**: % with annual A1C test
2. **A1C Control**: % with A1C <7%
3. **Nephropathy Screening**: % with urine test for protein
4. **Eye Exam**: % with annual dilated eye exam
5. **Medical Attention for Injury/Illness to Foot**: % with documented foot exam
6. **Medication Management**: % on appropriate diabetes medications

**Your Role**: Close gaps for testing; intensify treatment for control

### Hypertension (Controlling High Blood Pressure)
**Measures**:
1. **BP Screening**: % with documented BP readings
2. **BP Control**: % with BP <140/90 mmHg
3. **Medication Use**: % on appropriate antihypertensives

**Your Role**: Monitor BP regularly; adjust meds to reach goal

### Preventive Care (Vaccine Measures, Screening)
**Measures**:
1. **Childhood Vaccines**: % of children with complete vaccinations
2. **Adult Vaccines**: % of adults with flu, pneumonia vaccines
3. **Cancer Screening**: % of eligible patients with colorectal, breast, cervical cancer screening
4. **Depression Screening**: % screened with PHQ-2 or similar tool annually

**Your Role**: Screen all patients; document findings; arrange follow-up

### Cardiovascular Disease
**Measures**:
1. **Aspirin Use**: % on aspirin therapy when appropriate
2. **Lipid Control**: % with LDL <100 mg/dL
3. **Post-MI Medication Use**: % on beta blockers, ACE inhibitors post-MI
4. **Cardiac Rehabilitation**: % referred to cardiac rehab post-event

**Your Role**: Ensure medications; monitor lipid levels; refer when appropriate

### Mental Health
**Measures**:
1. **Depression Screening**: % screened annually (using validated tool)
2. **Antidepressant Treatment Follow-up**: % on antidepressants with follow-up visit
3. **Behavioral Health Integration**: % of patients screened for mental health/substance use issues

**Your Role**: Screen routinely; document; refer or treat appropriately

## Performance Comparisons

### Peer Benchmarking
1. From **Quality Metrics** view, click **Compare to Peers**
2. See your performance vs. other physicians in your organization
3. Shows (with anonymity protection):
   - High performers (top 10%)
   - Your performance
   - Low performers (bottom 10%)
   - Organizational average

**What This Shows**:
- Whether you're above/below organizational average
- Where you have biggest opportunity to improve
- What's possible (see high performers)

**How to Use**:
- Identify metrics where you lag peers
- Request peer mentoring from high performers
- Learn best practices from those doing better
- Set realistic improvement targets

### Trend Analysis
1. From metric detail page, click **Trend Chart**
2. See your 12-month performance history
3. Identifies:
   - Improvement trend (good: line going up)
   - Decline trend (concerning: line going down)
   - Seasonal patterns (worse winter, better summer)
   - Impact of interventions you made

**Interpreting Trends**:
- **Improving trend**: Your recent changes are working; continue approach
- **Declining trend**: Need to adjust strategy; something isn't working
- **Flat trend**: Metric stuck; need different approach
- **Seasonal pattern**: Account for this in planning

## Using Metrics for Improvement

### Monthly Metric Review
Recommended process:

1. **Schedule Time**: 20-30 minutes monthly
2. **Identify Opportunities**:
   - Which metrics most impactful? (largest gap to benchmark)
   - Which have most patients affected? (biggest denominator)
   - Which are you declining in? (negative trend)
3. **Prioritize**: Pick 1-2 metrics to focus on
4. **Plan Actions**:
   - What specifically will you do?
   - Who else needs to be involved?
   - What resources needed?
   - Timeline for improvement?
5. **Document**: Track what you're doing
6. **Follow Up**: Check progress in 30 days

### Common Improvement Strategies

**Strategy 1: Close Care Gaps**
- Identify patients who aren't meeting metric
- Complete the recommended service
- Most direct approach to improving process metrics

**Example**: A1C Control metric
- Identify all diabetic patients with A1C >7%
- Discuss increased medication with patient
- Order more frequent monitoring
- Follow up in 3 months

**Strategy 2: Improve Documentation**
- Service provided but not documented
- Go back and document what was done
- Helps meet metric even if service already done

**Example**: Depression Screening
- Patient already discussed depression
- Document that screening was completed (use PHQ-2 or note)
- Metric improves without additional work

**Strategy 3: Intensify Treatment**
- Patients having service but outcomes not met
- Increase intensity of therapy

**Example**: Blood Pressure Control
- Patient on one blood pressure medication
- Increase dose or add second agent
- Recheck BP in 2-4 weeks
- Goal is to get BP <140/90

**Strategy 4: Patient Education & Adherence**
- Service done but patient not following through
- Improve patient understanding and compliance

**Example**: Diabetes Self-Management
- Patient on insulin but A1C still high
- Review injection technique
- Assess diet/exercise compliance
- Refer to diabetes educator

**Strategy 5: Care Coordination**
- Complex care needs multi-provider coordination
- Improve communication and follow-up

**Example**: Post-Hospitalization Follow-up
- Patient discharged from hospital
- Ensure follow-up appointment scheduled
- Coordinate care between hospital and primary care
- Verify medications reconciled

## Quality Improvement Projects

### Launching a Focused Initiative

If one metric needs significant improvement:

1. **Assemble Team**:
   - You (physician)
   - Care manager (patient contact)
   - Clinical staff (implementation)
   - Scheduler (appointment coordination)

2. **Analyze Problem**:
   - How many patients missing this metric?
   - Why are they missing it? (barriers)
   - Which patients should you prioritize?

3. **Design Intervention**:
   - What will you actually do?
   - Who's responsible for each step?
   - What resources needed?
   - How will you track success?

4. **Implement**:
   - Start small (pilot with subset of patients)
   - Test approach with 10-20 patients first
   - Adjust as needed
   - Scale up if working

5. **Measure Results**:
   - Track improvement weekly/monthly
   - Adjust approach if not working
   - Celebrate progress
   - Document what worked

### Example Quality Improvement Project

**Goal**: Improve Depression Screening Rate from 45% to 75% in 6 months

**Problem Analysis**:
- Many patients aren't being screened
- No standardized screening tool in workflow
- Providers unsure how to document
- Follow-up pathways unclear

**Intervention**:
1. **Workflow Change**: Add PHQ-2 screening to rooming checklist
2. **Staff Training**: Teach MA how to administer PHQ-2
3. **Documentation**: Create template for entering screening result
4. **Follow-up Process**: Clear protocol for patients screening positive
5. **Monitoring**: Weekly tracking of screening completion

**Results Tracking**:
- Week 1-2: 50% screening (baseline + quick wins)
- Week 3-4: 58% screening (workflow working)
- Week 5-8: 72% screening (approaching goal)
- Month 6: 78% screening (exceeded goal)

## Common Metric Questions

### "My Metric Went Down - What Happened?"
**Causes**:
1. New patients added to panel (may not meet metric yet)
2. Your care declined (less likely if you've been consistent)
3. Data entry or documentation missed
4. Metric methodology changed
5. Seasonal variation

**Solution**:
1. Check denominator - did panel size change?
2. Review recent care - consistent with before?
3. Check documentation - were services documented?
4. Review metric definition - did it change?
5. Give it time - some changes lag data system

### "My Denominator Changed - Why?"
Denominator changes when:
1. New patients added to your panel (increases denominator)
2. Patients age out of metric (e.g., turn 75 for some metrics)
3. New diagnoses identified (e.g., newly diagnosed diabetics added)
4. Patients die or leave practice (decreases denominator)

**Impact**: Larger denominator makes metric harder to meet percentage-wise.
- Example: Was 90% with 20 patients (18/20)
- Add 10 new patients = now need 27/30 to reach 90%
- You haven't declined; denominator grew

### "Why Am I Below Benchmark?"
**Possible Reasons**:
1. Panel composition: Your patients sicker/older (higher baseline)
2. Baseline performance: You started lower
3. Your care quality: Genuinely lower than peers (focus for improvement)
4. Documentation: Services delivered but not documented
5. Timing: New interventions take time to show impact

**Approach**:
1. Understand why (ask colleagues doing better)
2. Set realistic improvement goal (not trying to reach 100%, start with 5% improvement)
3. Pick one actionable strategy
4. Give it 3 months to show results
5. Assess and adjust approach

## Metric Reporting and Accountability

### Who Sees Your Metrics
Depending on your organization:
- **You**: Full access to all your metrics
- **Your Medical Director**: Sees your performance for oversight
- **Quality Team**: Uses metrics for improvement support
- **Patients**: May see aggregated data (not identified)
- **Public (Sometimes)**: Medicare/CMS publishes some metrics

### Performance Expectations
Understand your organization's expectations:
- What metrics are you held accountable for?
- What's the expected performance level?
- What improvement trajectory expected?
- Consequences for underperformance?
- Incentives for high performance?

### Feedback and Support
If underperforming:
1. Expect feedback from medical director
2. Organization should offer support (coaching, resources)
3. Work with quality team on improvement plan
4. Regular check-ins on progress
5. Reassessment after improvement period

## Troubleshooting Metric Issues

### "My Metric Looks Wrong - Data Seems Incorrect"
**Solution**:
1. Check denominator (right patients included?)
2. Spot-check numerator (verify individual patients' data)
3. Compare to your documentation (does system match what you recorded?)
4. Report to quality team with specific examples
5. Request data validation review

### "I Did the Service But Metric Didn't Improve"
**Causes**:
1. Services not documented in system
2. Documentation in wrong location
3. EHR not synced with quality system
4. Metric uses different time period than you think
5. Data lag (may take 30-60 days to update)

**Solution**:
1. Verify documentation is complete/correct
2. Check with quality team on data syncing
3. Allow time for data updates (usually monthly)
4. Ask quality team to manually verify specific patients

### "Quality Team Says I'm Not Meeting Standard - I Disagree"
**Approach**:
1. Get specific data (which patients, what metric)
2. Review your documentation of care provided
3. Discuss with quality team (may be documentation issue)
4. If clinical disagreement, escalate to medical director
5. Request formal review if needed
